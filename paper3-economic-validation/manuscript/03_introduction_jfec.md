# 1. Introduction

Latent regime models have demonstrated empirical success in volatility forecasting. Our companion paper [@lim2026latent] establishes that inferring regime states from data, rather than conditioning on observable proxies, yields measurable improvements in forecast accuracy across a diverse asset panel. These latent regimes, identified through deep state-space models, consistently outperform specifications that rely on threshold variables such as lagged volatility or the VIX index. A second companion paper [@lim2026regime] addresses the evaluation challenge that regime-switching models pose for standard forecast comparison methods, developing regime-conditional proper scoring rules that reveal how unconditional metrics such as QLIKE [@patton2011volatility] can systematically misrank models whose performance varies across latent states. Together, the first paper establishes the inference methodology for latent volatility regimes while the second provides the evaluation framework necessary to assess them without averaging away economically meaningful heterogeneity.

Neither paper, however, addresses what these regimes represent. The latent state is constructed to optimize forecasting performance, not to correspond to any particular economic phenomenon. It is inferred from realized volatility data alone, without reference to funding conditions [@brunnermeier2009market], risk premia [@bollerslev2009expected], or other indicators of market stress. The regime variable improves predictions, but whether this improvement reflects genuine economic structure or merely statistical regularity remains an open question. A forecasting model can be useful without being interpretable, but the scientific value of latent regimes depends on whether they capture something beyond in-sample fit.

This paper examines whether latent volatility regimes exhibit external economic coherence. The research question is precise: do regime states, constructed without reference to external stress indicators, nonetheless align systematically with independent measures of market conditions? Alignment here refers to temporal correspondence, predictive relationships, and behavioral consistency across stress episodes. We do not ask whether regimes cause market outcomes or whether they can be given a single economic interpretation. We ask only whether they exhibit patterns consistent with economically meaningful variation.

The approach relies on strict separation between construction and validation. The latent regimes are taken as given from the companion papers [@lim2026latent; @lim2026regime], inferred from realized volatility and returns data. The validation variables---funding liquidity measures [@brunnermeier2009market], volatility risk premia [@bollerslev2009expected], correlation dynamics [@longin2001extreme], and crisis indicators---are held out entirely from regime construction. This separation prevents circular reasoning: we cannot claim that regimes capture funding stress if funding stress was used to identify them. The analysis examines lead-lag relationships, distributional differences across regime states, and behavior during known stress episodes. The objective is assessment, not explanation. We evaluate coherence without claiming to identify mechanisms.

The evidence supports economic coherence. The latent regime is contemporaneously aligned with implied volatility measures [@whaley2009understanding] and exhibits cross-correlation leads with credit spreads of 10 to 12 trading days, though controlled regressions indicate that this credit lead operates through the volatility channel rather than reflecting independent predictive content. The regime's incremental value emerges most clearly in anticipating yield curve dynamics and volatility-of-volatility shifts, where predictive content survives after controlling for realized volatility. Distributional separation across regime states is large, with effect sizes reaching Cohen's $d = 1.47$ for the VIX, and all seven external variables reject distributional equality at $p < 0.001$. During four crisis episodes spanning 2018 to 2023, the regime activated before the volatility peak in every case and normalized faster than its observable counterpart, producing fewer false positive stress signals. These findings suggest that latent volatility regimes capture genuine economic structure rather than mere statistical regularity.
