# 7. Illustrative Case Studies: Design and Purpose

This section describes the design and rationale for the case study illustrations that complement the systematic analyses. Case studies are not the primary evidence for regime coherence; that role belongs to the statistical analyses presented in the results. Rather, case studies provide concrete illustrations that make abstract patterns tangible.

## 7.1 Purpose of Case Studies

Case studies served three functions in this analysis.

First, they provided illustration. Statistical summaries compress information. A correlation coefficient or mean difference obscures the temporal dynamics that produced it. Case studies decompress this information, showing how regimes evolved during specific episodes. This aided interpretation and built intuition for the regime dynamics documented in the formal results.

Second, they provided transparency. Case studies allowed readers to assess regime behavior directly. Rather than accepting statistical summaries, readers could examine whether regime trajectories during known events appeared sensible. This transparency supports critical evaluation of the quantitative findings.

Third, they served communication. For non-specialist audiences, case studies are more accessible than formal statistical results. A figure showing regime evolution during a well-known crisis conveys information that regression coefficients cannot.

## 7.2 What Case Studies Are Not

Case studies did not accomplish several things that merit explicit acknowledgment. They are not evidence: case studies selected after observing results invite cherry-picking, and even with pre-specification, a handful of episodes cannot establish generality. The statistical analyses provided the evidence; case studies illustrated. They are not discovery: the analyses were pre-specified, and case studies visualized pre-specified comparisons rather than generating new tests. They are not causal identification: narrative accounts of "why" regimes moved during episodes remain speculation. The case studies described what happened, not why it happened.

## 7.3 Episode Selection Criteria

Case study episodes were selected based on four criteria. First, ex ante significance: episodes had to be widely recognized as significant stress events, identifiable without reference to regime behavior, since selection based on regime characteristics would have been circular. Second, data availability: the full set of external variables had to be available during the episode, excluding episodes predating key data series. Third, diversity: episodes spanned different types of stress, including funding crises, volatility events, and macroeconomic shocks. Fourth, recency: some episodes were recent to demonstrate relevance to current market conditions.

## 7.4 Selected Episodes

Four episodes were selected for case study analysis. The analysis originally specified five episodes including the Global Financial Crisis and the European Sovereign Debt Crisis, but these were excluded from the final crisis timing analysis due to data availability constraints for certain external variables. The four episodes analyzed were:

### 7.4.1 Volmageddon (February 2018)

A short, sharp volatility event triggered by the collapse of short-volatility products. The VIX spiked above 50 before rapidly normalizing. This episode tested whether regimes distinguished transient volatility spikes from sustained stress. Key dates included the VIX spike on February 5, 2018, the XIV termination on February 6, and normalization by late February.

### 7.4.2 COVID-19 Market Disruption (March 2020)

The fastest equity market decline in history, accompanied by severe dislocations in Treasury and credit markets and an unprecedented policy response. This episode tested regime behavior during an exogenous, non-financial shock. Key dates included the WHO pandemic declaration on March 11, 2020, market circuit breakers on March 9, 12, and 16, Fed emergency actions from March 15 through 23, and the market trough on March 23.

### 7.4.3 2022 Rate Shock

A prolonged adjustment to Federal Reserve tightening, featuring multiple VIX spikes without a single crisis peak. This episode tested regime behavior during extended stress without a clear focal point. Key dates included the first 2022 rate hike in March, the start of 75 basis point hikes in June, the UK gilt crisis in September, and year-end normalization.

### 7.4.4 SVB Crisis (March 2023)

The failure of Silicon Valley Bank triggered a brief but intense period of financial stress, particularly in regional banking stocks and credit markets. This episode tested regime behavior during a sector-specific shock with potential systemic implications. Key dates centered on the FDIC seizure of SVB on March 10, 2023, and the subsequent week of contagion concerns.

## 7.5 Analysis Protocol

For each episode, a standardized analysis protocol was applied. Timelines were constructed showing the daily regime indicator alongside primary external variables and key event markers. The observable regime indicator $G_{\text{obs}}$ was compared alongside $G_{\text{ssm}}$ to assess whether the latent indicator exhibited smoother dynamics, fewer false reversals, or different activation timing. After examining individual episodes, regime behavior was compared across episodes to assess consistency in peak levels, activation timing, and recovery dynamics. These comparisons were visual and qualitative; formal statistical tests were reserved for the systematic analyses.

## 7.6 Presentation Format

Case studies were presented with standardized figures: regime timelines showing the indicator over the episode window with event markers, multi-panel comparisons of regime and external variables, and a cross-episode summary with regime trajectories from all episodes overlaid and centered on event time. Figures were accompanied by brief narrative descriptions stating what the figure showed, with narratives avoiding causal interpretation and describing patterns without explaining mechanisms.

## 7.7 Limitations of Case Study Evidence

Several limitations applied. The selected episodes, even with pre-specification, may not represent the full distribution of stress events, and conclusions may not generalize to future episodes with different characteristics. Hindsight bias was an inherent concern: we knew how each episode resolved, and this knowledge may have influenced interpretation even when we attempted neutrality. Four episodes provided limited information; consistent patterns were suggestive but not definitive, and inconsistent patterns may have reflected genuine heterogeneity rather than regime failure. Finally, case studies invited narrative construction, and the commitment to descriptive, pattern-focused presentation was maintained throughout. These limitations reinforced the subordinate role of case studies: they illustrated and communicated, but they did not prove.
