# 3. What "Economic Meaning" Means (and Does Not Mean)

The phrase "economic meaning" invites misunderstanding. It suggests that latent variables can be assigned definitive interpretations, that we can state with confidence what the regime state "is" in economic terms. This section clarifies the limited sense in which we use this phrase and explicitly disclaims stronger interpretations.

## 3.1 This Paper Does Not Identify Causal Mechanisms

We state this directly: this paper does not identify the economic mechanisms that generate latent regime dynamics. We do not claim that funding stress causes regime transitions, that risk appetite shifts drive regime states, or that any particular macroeconomic factor explains regime variation. Causal identification requires experimental variation or quasi-experimental designs that are not available in our setting.

The analysis examines correlations, lead-lag relationships, and distributional patterns. These are descriptive statistics that characterize associations. Association is not causation, and no amount of sophisticated analysis converts one into the other without identifying assumptions that we are not prepared to defend.

## 3.2 What "Meaning" Refers To

When we ask whether latent regimes have "economic meaning," we refer to three specific properties:

**Temporal coherence.** The regime state should exhibit persistent dynamics consistent with economic regime phenomena. Rapid oscillation between states would suggest noise rather than genuine regime structure. Smooth transitions and sustained periods in each state are consistent with meaningful variation.

**Predictive alignment.** The regime state should relate systematically to economic indicators that were not used in its construction. This alignment should be robust across indicators and time periods. Isolated correlations with cherry-picked variables would not constitute meaningful alignment; consistent patterns across multiple stress proxies would.

**Stability across episodes.** The regime state should behave consistently during known stress episodes. If the regime is elevated during one crisis but not another of comparable severity, this inconsistency undermines the interpretation. Stability does not require identical responses---crises differ in nature---but patterns should be recognizable across episodes.

These properties are necessary but not sufficient for economic meaning. A variable could exhibit all three and still reflect statistical artifacts rather than economic fundamentals. But absence of these properties would provide strong evidence against meaningful interpretation.

## 3.3 Why Over-Interpretation Is Dangerous

The temptation to over-interpret latent variables is substantial. A regime state that aligns with stress indicators invites narratives: "the regime captures investor fear," "the regime reflects funding conditions," "the regime identifies systemic risk." These narratives are appealing because they provide intuition for otherwise abstract statistical objects.

The danger is twofold. First, over-interpretation invites falsification. If we claim the regime "captures investor fear," we commit to specific predictions about behavior that may not hold. The regime might align with fear proxies during one period and diverge during another, undermining the interpretation. Second, over-interpretation obscures uncertainty. The regime may reflect multiple, partially correlated phenomena; assigning it a single interpretation suppresses this complexity.

More practically, over-interpretation invites criticism. Econometrics examiners are trained to identify unsupported causal claims. Thesis committees probe the gap between evidence and interpretation. A conservative interpretation that acknowledges limitations is more defensible than a bold interpretation that overstates conclusions.

## 3.4 The Role of Multiple Indicators

Our validation strategy uses multiple external indicators precisely to avoid over-interpretation. If the regime aligned with a single stress proxy, we could not distinguish genuine meaning from spurious correlation. The proxy might share common noise with volatility data, producing mechanical association without economic content.

Multiple indicators from different categories---funding, risk premia, correlation, event-based---provide triangulation. If the regime aligns with indicators across categories, the alignment is less likely to reflect idiosyncratic measurement issues. The common element across indicators is the hypothesized regime structure, not shared noise.

This does not prove economic meaning. All indicators might share common confounders with volatility that drive spurious alignment. But multiple-indicator alignment is more consistent with genuine meaning than single-indicator correlation.

## 3.5 What We Can and Cannot Conclude

Given the evidence that will be presented, the strongest supportable conclusions are of the form:

- "The latent regime exhibits systematic alignment with multiple stress indicators"
- "Regime transitions are temporally associated with changes in funding conditions"
- "High-regime periods are characterized by distributional properties consistent with market stress"

These statements describe patterns without claiming causation or structural interpretation. They support the view that regimes capture economically relevant variation without specifying what that variation represents.

Conclusions we cannot support, regardless of the evidence:

- "The regime measures systemic risk"
- "The regime identifies market stress"
- "The regime reflects investor sentiment"

These statements imply equivalence or identity that the evidence cannot establish. The regime may be correlated with these concepts without being reducible to any of them.
