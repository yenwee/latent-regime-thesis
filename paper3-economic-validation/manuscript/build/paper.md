title: "What Do Latent Volatility Regimes Represent? External Validation with Held-Out Stress Indicators"
author:
  - "Yen Wee Lim, University of Malaya, Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia"
date: "March 2026"
abstract: |
  Latent regime models have demonstrated empirical success in volatility forecasting, yet whether the inferred regime states correspond to economically meaningful market conditions remains an open question. This paper examines the external coherence of latent volatility regimes---whether regime states, constructed from realized volatility data alone, exhibit systematic alignment with independent measures of market stress that were not used in their construction. Taking latent regimes as given from a companion paper [@lim2026latent] that develops deep state-space inference for asset-specific regime identification, we test alignment with held-out funding liquidity measures, volatility risk premia, and crisis indicators across a 21-asset panel spanning 2017--2025. The latent regime exhibits contemporaneous alignment with VIX (Cohen's $d = 1.47$), leads credit spreads by 10--12 trading days, and activates before all four crisis episodes examined (up to 15 days before the COVID crash peak), though controlled regressions indicate that the credit lead operates through the volatility channel rather than reflecting independent predictive content. The latent indicator normalizes 2--11 days faster than its observable counterpart during all crisis episodes, consistent with fewer false positive stress signals. The regime's genuine incremental value lies in anticipating yield curve dynamics (term spread prediction significant at all horizons after controlling for realized volatility, $p < 0.013$) and capturing variance risk premium shifts (VVIX Granger causality significant at all lag orders). These findings support the interpretation that latent volatility regimes capture genuine economic structure rather than mere statistical regularity.
