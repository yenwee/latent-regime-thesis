# 7. Results

This section presents the empirical findings from each component of the validation analysis. The results are organized to follow the empirical strategy: lead-lag structure, distributional differences across regime states, crisis episode analysis, predictive content beyond volatility, and the comparison between observable and latent regime indicators. Throughout, we report specific magnitudes and effect sizes alongside statistical significance, consistent with the emphasis on economic substance over mechanical hypothesis testing.

## 7.1 Lead-Lag Structure

Cross-correlation analysis reveals that the latent regime indicator $G_{\text{ssm}}$ [@lim2026latent] exhibits contemporaneous alignment with equity volatility measures and a leading relationship with credit and funding variables. Table 1 reports the peak cross-correlation, peak lag, and contemporaneous correlation for each external variable. The regime indicator is contemporaneously correlated with VIX [@whaley2009understanding] ($\rho = 0.254$, $p < 0.001$) and VVIX [@huang2019vvix] ($\rho = 0.198$, $p < 0.001$), indicating that latent regime states and implied volatility measures share information at the daily frequency. The CBOE SKEW index exhibits a contemporaneous negative correlation ($\rho = -0.257$, $p = 0.009$), consistent with the interpretation that high-regime periods coincide with reduced demand for out-of-the-money call options relative to puts [@bakshi2003stock].

For credit spread variables [@gilchrist2012credit], the peak correlation occurs at positive lags, indicating that regime activation precedes widening in credit markets. High-yield spreads (HY OAS) reach peak correlation at a lag of 12 trading days ($\rho = 0.180$, $p = 0.018$), while investment-grade BBB spreads peak at 10 days ($\rho = 0.191$, $p = 0.013$). The term spread exhibits peak correlation at a negative lag of 20 days ($\rho = 0.191$, $p = 0.012$), suggesting that yield curve movements precede regime activation, consistent with the view that monetary policy signals propagate to volatility with a delay. The TED spread shows peak correlation at 16 days ($\rho = 0.205$), though this relationship does not achieve conventional significance ($p = 0.211$), reflecting the shorter and noisier sample available for this variable. Figure 1 displays the full cross-correlation functions with confidence bands, illustrating the smooth decay of correlations at increasing lags and the asymmetric lead-lag profile across variable categories.

![](figures/fig1_cross_correlation.pdf)

*Figure 1: Cross-correlation functions between $G_{\text{ssm}}$ and external stress variables for the S\&P 500 at lags $k \in [-20, +20]$ trading days. Positive lags indicate the regime leading the external variable. Shaded bands represent 95\% Newey-West confidence intervals. Peak lag annotated for each variable.*

Granger causality tests [@granger1969investigating] provide a complementary assessment of predictive relationships. Of the seven external variables examined, five exhibit statistically significant Granger causality from the regime indicator at conventional levels. The strongest evidence appears for VVIX, where the regime Granger-causes subsequent movements at all lag orders from 1 through 5 ($F = 10.20$, $p = 0.001$ at lag 1). Investment-grade credit spreads show significant Granger causality at lag 1 ($F = 9.80$, $p = 0.002$), and the term spread achieves significance at all tested lag orders ($F = 8.81$, $p = 0.003$ at lag 1). The TED spread exhibits significant causality at lags 1, 2, and 5, while the SKEW index reaches significance at lag 5 ($F = 2.40$, $p = 0.035$). The VIX narrowly misses conventional significance at lag 1 ($F = 3.51$, $p = 0.061$), which is unsurprising given that the contemporaneous relationship is strong---predictive content at daily frequency may largely be absorbed within the same trading day. High-yield spreads similarly fall short of significance ($F = 2.68$, $p = 0.102$), possibly reflecting the lower-frequency dynamics of corporate bond markets relative to the daily regime indicator. The overall pattern---significant Granger causality for five of seven variables, with the two exceptions exhibiting marginal evidence---is consistent with the regime indicator containing forward-looking information about a range of stress-related variables. ## 7.2 Distributional Differences Across Regime States

The distributional analysis partitions the sample into high-regime ($G_{\text{ssm}} > 0.5$) and low-regime ($G_{\text{ssm}} \leq 0.5$) periods and compares the conditional distributions of each external variable. Table 2 reports conditional means, effect sizes, and formal test statistics. All Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests reject the null of distributional equality at $p < 0.001$, indicating that external variables exhibit materially different behavior across regime states.

Effect sizes, measured by Cohen's $d$, range from moderate to very large. The VIX exhibits the largest separation, with a mean of 25.67 in high-regime periods versus 14.89 in low-regime periods, yielding an effect size of $d = 1.47$. This magnitude exceeds typical benchmarks for "very large" effects and indicates that regime classification captures substantial variation in implied volatility levels. VVIX displays an effect size of $d = 0.98$ (mean 110.9 vs. 94.5), and the SKEW index shows $d = -0.98$ (mean 130.1 vs. 143.0, with the sign reflecting the inverse relationship between stress and the SKEW index). Among credit variables, high-yield spreads exhibit $d = 0.83$ (4.32 vs. 3.46 percentage points) and BBB spreads show $d = 0.80$ (1.65 vs. 1.29 percentage points). The term spread and TED spread display moderate effect sizes of $d = 0.63$ and $d = 0.52$, respectively.

Beyond mean differences, the variance ratios indicate substantially greater dispersion during high-regime periods. The VIX exhibits a variance ratio of 10.21, meaning that implied volatility is approximately ten times more variable during high-regime periods. Credit spreads show even more concentrated heteroscedasticity, with HY OAS and BBB OAS variance ratios of 6.88 and 7.19, respectively. The TED spread variance ratio of 8.58 is similarly elevated. These patterns are consistent with the interpretation that high-regime periods correspond to states of market stress in which both the level and volatility of stress indicators increase. The SKEW index is a notable exception, with a variance ratio near unity (1.03) and an insignificant Levene test ($p = 0.287$), suggesting that while the mean of the skew distribution shifts across regimes, its dispersion does not. Figure 3 displays the effect sizes as a grouped bar chart, making the separation visually apparent across all variables.

## 7.3 Crisis Episode Analysis

The crisis episode analysis examines regime behavior during four pre-specified stress events: the February 2018 Volmageddon episode, the March 2020 COVID crash, the September 2022 rate shock, and the March 2023 SVB crisis. Table 3 reports timing metrics for the S&P 500 regime indicator and the panel mean across available assets.

The COVID crash provides the most striking evidence of anticipatory regime behavior. For the S&P 500, $G_{\text{ssm}}$ first activated 15 trading days before the volatility peak, reaching a maximum of 0.69 while remaining elevated for 24 days. The panel mean across assets shows similar early activation (14.5 days before peak), with a higher panel-average peak of 0.93, reflecting the broad cross-sectional impact of the pandemic shock. This lead time is economically meaningful: 15 trading days represents approximately three calendar weeks of advance signal relative to the most extreme volatility realization. The 2022 rate shock exhibits the longest lead time, with $G_{\text{ssm}}$ activating 30 days before the volatility peak for the S&P 500 (panel mean: 23.7 days). However, the peak regime level was more moderate (0.56 for S&P 500), consistent with the characterization of this episode as a prolonged adjustment rather than an acute crisis.

The two shorter episodes---Volmageddon and SVB---display markedly different dynamics. Both show regime activation only one day before the volatility peak for the S&P 500, consistent with the sudden, event-driven nature of these shocks. Peak regime values were moderate (0.75 and 0.52, respectively), and the regime normalized rapidly (5 and 2 days elevated). The brief duration of elevated regime states during these transient events is itself informative: the latent indicator distinguished between short-lived volatility spikes and sustained stress episodes, with COVID and the rate shock producing regime persistence roughly five to twelve times longer than Volmageddon or SVB. Figure 4 overlays the regime trajectories for all four episodes, centered on event time, illustrating the heterogeneous dynamics across crisis types.

![](figures/fig4_crisis_episodes.pdf)

*Figure 4: Regime indicator trajectories during four crisis episodes, centered on event peak ($t = 0$). Green solid: $G_{\text{ssm}}$ (latent). Blue dashed: $G_{\text{obs}}$ (observable). Vertical lines indicate episode start and end dates.*

A consistent finding across all episodes is that $G_{\text{ssm}}$ normalizes more rapidly than its observable counterpart $G_{\text{obs}}$. During Volmageddon, the latent indicator remained elevated for 5 days compared to 16 for the observable indicator, a difference of 11 days. During COVID, the latent indicator was elevated for 24 days versus 32 for the observable (a difference of 8 days). The rate shock and SVB episodes showed smaller but consistent differences of 2 and 4 days, respectively. The panel means confirm this pattern, with the latent indicator spending fewer days in the elevated state across all four episodes (4.7 vs. 9.7, 25.2 vs. 28.1, 12.1 vs. 19.0, and 5.6 vs. 8.7 days for Volmageddon, COVID, the rate shock, and SVB, respectively). This faster normalization is consistent with the latent regime producing fewer false positive stress signals---returning to the low state more promptly once acute stress subsides.

## 7.4 Predictive Content Beyond Volatility

The predictive regression analysis tests whether the latent regime indicator contains information about future values of stress variables that is not already captured by realized volatility. The specification includes contemporaneous realized volatility as a control, ensuring that any predictive content attributed to the regime is incremental to what observable volatility provides. Table 4 reports the regression coefficients, standard errors, and incremental $R^2$ for three target variables at forecast horizons of 1, 5, and 22 trading days.

The term spread emerges as the variable for which regime predictive content is most robust. The regime coefficient is statistically significant at all three horizons: $\beta = -0.029$ ($t = -3.12$, $p = 0.002$) at $h = 1$, $\beta = -0.112$ ($t = -3.01$, $p = 0.003$) at $h = 5$, and $\beta = -0.329$ ($t = -2.50$, $p = 0.013$) at $h = 22$. The negative sign indicates that high-regime states predict subsequent compression of the term spread, consistent with a flight-to-safety interpretation in which elevated stress leads to flattening or inversion of the yield curve. The incremental $R^2$ grows with the forecast horizon, from 0.001 percent at $h = 1$ to 0.014 percent at $h = 5$ and 0.12 percent at $h = 22$. These magnitudes are small in absolute terms, which is expected given the strong persistence of the term spread and the already high baseline $R^2$ of the control model (0.997 at $h = 1$, declining to 0.926 at $h = 22$). The systematic increase in incremental predictive content at longer horizons suggests that regime information pertains to medium-term yield curve dynamics rather than daily fluctuations.

The VIX shows significant regime predictive content at the one-day horizon ($\beta = -1.62$, $t = -3.41$, $p < 0.001$) and marginal significance at $h = 5$ ($\beta = -1.69$, $t = -1.70$, $p = 0.089$), but the relationship dissipates at the monthly horizon ($p = 0.672$). The negative coefficient at short horizons may appear counterintuitive, since high regimes are associated with elevated VIX levels. However, once contemporaneous realized volatility is controlled for, the negative sign reflects the mean-reverting tendency of implied volatility: conditional on current RV, a high regime state predicts modest VIX decline, consistent with the resolution of the volatility risk premium. High-yield spreads do not exhibit significant predictive content from the regime at any horizon, with the strongest evidence appearing at $h = 22$ ($t = 1.32$, $p = 0.186$). This null result may reflect the slow-moving nature of credit markets relative to the daily regime indicator. Figure 5 displays the incremental $R^2$ across horizons for each target variable.

![](figures/fig5_predictive_r2.pdf)

*Figure 5: Incremental $R^2$ from adding $G_{\text{ssm}}$ to the baseline regression (RV + lagged outcome) for predicting future stress variables at horizons $h = 1, 5, 22$ trading days.*

The panel predictive analysis extends these results to 20 assets. Table 5 reports the fraction of assets for which the regime coefficient is significant, the fraction with the expected sign, and the mean incremental $R^2$. At the 22-day horizon, 20 percent of assets show significant regime predictive content for VIX, with 80 percent displaying positive coefficients and a mean incremental $R^2$ of 0.25 percent. For high-yield spreads at $h = 22$, 15 percent of assets are significant, 80 percent positive, and the mean incremental $R^2$ is 0.13 percent. These panel results indicate that the predictive content observed for the S&P 500 is not idiosyncratic; it appears, with varying strength, across the broader asset panel.

## 7.5 Observable vs. Latent Regime Comparison

The comparison between $G_{\text{obs}}$ (the observable transition function from the STR-HAR model) and $G_{\text{ssm}}$ (the VRNN-inferred latent regime) provides a direct test of discriminant validity [@campbell1959convergent]: whether the additional complexity of latent state inference yields a cleaner stress signal than the simpler observable conditioning approach. Table 2 permits this comparison through the distributional effect sizes for both indicators.

The latent indicator achieves larger Cohen's $d$ values for four of the seven external variables, with the most pronounced advantages appearing for VVIX ($d = 0.98$ vs. $0.66$, a difference of $+0.32$) and the term spread ($d = 0.63$ vs. $0.43$, $+0.20$). For the VIX, the latent indicator also shows a meaningful advantage ($d = 1.47$ vs. $1.30$, $+0.17$), and a modest edge appears for the TED spread ($d = 0.52$ vs. $0.46$, $+0.06$). The observable indicator, by contrast, produces slightly larger effect sizes for the SKEW index ($d = 1.08$ vs. $0.98$, $+0.10$), high-yield spreads ($d = 0.86$ vs. $0.83$, $+0.03$), and BBB spreads ($d = 0.86$ vs. $0.80$, $+0.06$). The credit spread advantages for the observable indicator are small and may reflect the mechanical relationship between the observable threshold variable (lagged realized volatility or VIX) and credit conditions. Overall, the latent regime achieves an average absolute Cohen's $d$ of 0.89 across all seven variables, compared to 0.81 for the observable regime.

The crisis timing comparison reinforces the distinction between the two indicators. As noted in Section 7.3, $G_{\text{ssm}}$ normalizes faster than $G_{\text{obs}}$ across all four episodes, spending fewer days in the elevated state by margins ranging from 2 to 11 days. This pattern is consistent with the latent indicator capturing a cleaner demarcation between stress and non-stress states: it activates during genuine stress and returns to baseline more promptly when stress subsides, whereas the observable indicator, being more directly tied to recent volatility realizations, tends to linger in the elevated state as volatility gradually decays. The peak regime values are systematically lower for $G_{\text{ssm}}$ than for $G_{\text{obs}}$ across all episodes (e.g., 0.69 vs. 1.00 during COVID for S&P 500), reflecting a more conservative classification that nonetheless maintains earlier or equivalent activation timing. Figure 6 presents the peak alignment comparison across all stress variables.

![](figures/fig6_obs_vs_latent_alignment.pdf)

*Figure 6: Peak absolute cross-correlation ($|\rho|$) for $G_{\text{obs}}$ (blue) versus $G_{\text{ssm}}$ (green) across all seven external stress variables.*

The combined evidence from distributional analysis and crisis timing supports the interpretation that the latent regime provides a qualitatively different, and in several respects sharper, characterization of market stress than the observable alternative. The advantage is not uniform---credit spreads respond similarly to both indicators---but it is systematic for volatility-related measures and for the speed of normalization following stress events.

## 7.6 Robustness

The robustness analysis examines whether the principal findings are sensitive to the regime classification threshold. The baseline analysis uses a threshold of $G > 0.5$ to partition high and low regime states; alternative thresholds of 0.3 (liberal) and 0.7 (conservative) are evaluated. Distributional effect sizes remain qualitatively stable across thresholds, though their magnitudes shift in the expected direction: the conservative threshold (0.7) produces larger effect sizes for most variables, as the more extreme classification isolates periods of more pronounced stress, while the liberal threshold (0.3) attenuates effect sizes by including more marginal observations in the high-regime category. The ranking of variables by effect size is preserved across all three thresholds, and no variable loses statistical significance at the $p < 0.001$ level under either alternative. Figure 8 displays the threshold sensitivity for the primary distributional results.

![](figures/fig8_threshold_sensitivity.pdf)

*Figure 8: Threshold sensitivity analysis. Mean Cohen's $d$ across external variables as a function of regime quantile split. Traces show individual variables; bold line shows the cross-variable mean.*

The lead-lag structure is similarly robust. The qualitative pattern---contemporaneous alignment with volatility measures, leading relationship with credit spreads---is preserved regardless of the threshold used for distributional partitioning, since the cross-correlation analysis operates on the continuous regime indicator rather than the discretized classification. The Granger causality results are likewise independent of the distributional threshold. The crisis episode findings are inherently continuous, as the timing of regime activation and normalization is defined by the raw $G_{\text{ssm}}$ trajectory rather than a binary classification. Taken together, the robustness checks indicate that the principal conclusions are not artifacts of the particular threshold chosen for the baseline distributional analysis.
