# 7. Conclusion

This paper proposes the Deep-LSTR framework, integrating deep state-space models with Smooth Transition HAR specifications by using inferred latent states as transition variables. Evaluating 21 liquid assets across equity, fixed income, currency, and commodity markets from 2015-2025, we provide three contributions to the volatility forecasting literature.

First, we demonstrate that latent regime identification yields consistent improvements over observable-transition specifications across the majority of individual assets. Deep-LSTR achieves the lowest or near-lowest median QLIKE loss at all horizons, winning on 60--80% of assets depending on forecast horizon and achieving MCS inclusion rates of 89--100%. The improvement is consistent with a regime smoothing mechanism: the latent transition variable exhibits substantially lower volatility than observable proxies (standard deviation 0.21 versus 0.28) and remains closer to the regime threshold, reaching extreme values on only 19% of days compared to 34% for observable signals. This conservative regime identification is associated with fewer false positive regime switches---days when transient volatility spikes incorrectly indicate persistent regime changes---and our evidence suggests these properties contribute to consistent forecasting gains that compound over time. We note that mean QLIKE is sensitive to a small number of outlier assets where the VRNN latent state is poorly calibrated; median-based evaluation, which is robust to such outliers, confirms Deep-LSTR's advantage across the panel.

Second, we document that Deep-LSTR's advantage is broad-based rather than concentrated in specific asset classes. QLIKE improvements over HAR are modest in magnitude across all classes (0.0--0.4%), but win rates are high (50--100%) and MCS inclusion rates are 100% for equities, currencies, and commodities, though lower for fixed income (50%), where Treasury yield volatility (TNX, TYX) favors the observable-transition specification at the weekly horizon. This pattern---consistent gains across most markets with identifiable exceptions---is consistent with a general regime smoothing mechanism, while acknowledging that the VRNN's latent state inference is less effective for certain fixed income dynamics.

Third, we apply the Fissler-Ziegel joint elicitability framework for Expected Shortfall evaluation, achieving 1.5% lower FZ0 loss than HAR at the 1% VaR level while maintaining proper VaR calibration (Kupiec $p > 0.05$ for all models). While the FZ0 improvement is modest in unconditional magnitude, it is consistent across coverage levels and the full asset panel. This addresses the methodological gap whereby most deep-learning volatility studies employ invalid scoring rules for ES comparison, rendering their tail risk claims formally unfounded. As discussed in Section 5.4, unconditional risk metrics inherently dilute regime-dependent advantages by averaging over predominantly calm periods; the true economic value of latent regime identification for tail risk may be substantially larger during stress episodes.

The Deep-LSTR framework bridges deep probabilistic inference and interpretable econometrics, providing regime-dependent coefficients that can be examined and tested while leveraging latent state inference to filter noise from regime signals. Unlike pure black-box approaches, the STR-HAR structure preserves economic interpretability: practitioners can examine how volatility persistence parameters shift between regimes and validate that estimated dynamics accord with economic intuition. The regime smoothing mechanism—whereby the latent state appears to act as a de-noised regime indicator—represents a theoretically motivated interpretation of Deep-LSTR's forecasting gains, distinct from claims of anticipatory capacity.

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the linear projection from the multi-dimensional latent state to a scalar regime indicator ($s_t = \alpha^\top z_t$) constrains the regime to be a one-dimensional summary of the latent space. This is a deliberate design choice: it ensures compatibility with the STR-HAR framework and preserves interpretability of the transition function. However, it limits the regime's capacity to capture multi-dimensional latent structure. We emphasize that the VRNN learns $z_t$ freely via the ELBO objective---the projection is applied after inference, not during training---so the latent representation is not constrained to mimic any observable. The empirical evidence supports this distinction: the latent indicator normalizes 2 to 11 days faster than the observable proxy during crisis episodes, a property that a simple smoothed volatility measure cannot produce. Future work could explore nonlinear projection or end-to-end training that jointly optimizes latent inference and forecasting. Second, the deep state-space model requires more computational resources than standard HAR estimation. VRNN training requires approximately 10-20 minutes per asset on Apple M4 Pro hardware for the full training schedule, though early stopping typically terminates within 200-300 epochs. However, the annual retraining protocol limits this burden to once per year, after which daily forecasting requires only forward inference through the trained network (under 1 second per asset). For a 21-asset portfolio, annual model updating completes within 4-6 hours---comparable to sophisticated GARCH variants with numerical MLE optimization---and daily production forecasting adds negligible latency. Third, the two-dimensional latent space, while optimal in our robustness analysis, may not capture all relevant regime dynamics in more complex market environments. Fourth, our analysis focuses on univariate volatility forecasting; extending to multivariate settings with cross-asset spillovers remains for future work.

Future research directions include incorporating additional information sources such as options-implied volatility surfaces and high-frequency order flow data into the latent state inference. Alternative deep state-space architectures, including attention-based transformers and neural ordinary differential equations, may further improve regime identification. Finally, extending the framework to joint multivariate volatility and correlation forecasting would enhance portfolio risk management applications.
