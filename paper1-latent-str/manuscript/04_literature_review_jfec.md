# 2. Literature Review

## 2.1. Realized Volatility and the HAR Model

The development of high-frequency financial econometrics enabled direct observation of a quantity traditionally treated as latent. @andersen1998answering demonstrated that realized variance, computed as the sum of squared intraday returns, provides a consistent estimator of integrated variance under standard diffusion assumptions. This paradigm shift from latent-variable GARCH models to observable, model-free volatility measurement was formalized by @andersen2003modeling, who established three empirical regularities: realized volatility exhibits long memory with slowly decaying autocorrelations, its logarithm is approximately Gaussian, and large shocks occur more frequently than Gaussian predictions imply. The importance of accurate volatility measurement extends beyond forecasting to option pricing, portfolio allocation, and risk management [@poon2003forecasting].

@corsi2009simple introduced the HAR-RV model, which approximates long-memory dynamics through a simple additive cascade:
$$
RV_{t+H} = \beta_0 + \beta_d RV^{(d)}_t + \beta_w RV^{(w)}_t + \beta_m RV^{(m)}_t + \epsilon_{t+H}
$$
where $RV^{(d)}, RV^{(w)}, RV^{(m)}$ represent daily, weekly, and monthly realized volatility components. The theoretical foundation derives from the Heterogeneous Market Hypothesis [@muller1997volatilities], which posits that market participants operating on different time horizons generate distinct volatility components. The HAR model's parsimony and interpretability have made it the dominant benchmark in realized volatility forecasting.

The HAR framework has spawned numerous extensions addressing specific empirical phenomena. @andersen2007roughing introduced HAR-J separating continuous variation from jumps, recognizing that discontinuous price movements carry distinct information content. @corsi2010threshold extended this in HAR-TCJ with threshold-based jump detection. @bollerslev2016exploiting augmented HAR with realized quarticity in HAR-Q, exploiting measurement error heteroskedasticity. @patton2015good incorporated leverage effects through LHAR, decomposing returns into positive and negative components. @bekaert2014vix examined the relationship between realized and implied volatility within HAR frameworks. Despite these advances, none allows for the smooth, continuous coefficient variation that characterizes transitions between market regimes.

Recent work has challenged the traditional long-memory interpretation of volatility persistence. @gatheral2018volatility demonstrated that realized volatility exhibits "rough" behavior characterized by a Hurst exponent $H \approx 0.1$, far below the $H = 0.5$ threshold that would indicate standard Brownian motion. This roughness—evident in the power-law decay of volatility increment autocorrelations—suggests that volatility is locally more irregular than previously assumed while maintaining long-range dependence at larger scales. The rough volatility framework, formalized through fractional Brownian motion with $H < 0.5$, has profound implications for option pricing and volatility forecasting. @comte1998long provided theoretical foundations for long-memory stochastic volatility in continuous time, establishing conditions under which fractional integration arises from volatility of volatility dynamics. @bennedsen2022decoupling further developed this literature by proposing methods to decouple short-term roughness from long-term persistence using Brownian semistationary processes, demonstrating that volatility exhibits both properties simultaneously at different time scales. These findings motivate multi-scale approaches to volatility modeling and support the HAR framework's decomposition of volatility into components operating at different frequencies.

## 2.2. Regime-Switching in Volatility

Financial volatility exhibits well-documented nonlinearities that linear specifications cannot capture. The seminal work of @engle1982autoregressive established the Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity (ARCH) framework, demonstrating that volatility clustering—periods of high volatility followed by high volatility and low by low—can be captured through models where conditional variance depends on past squared residuals. This foundational contribution, recognized with the 2003 Nobel Prize in Economics, spawned the extensive GARCH literature [@bollerslev1986generalized] and established the paradigm of time-varying conditional variance that underlies all modern volatility modeling.

The leverage effect [@black1976studies; @christie1982stochastic] describes asymmetric responses whereby negative returns increase future volatility more than positive returns. Volatility feedback effects create self-exciting dynamics where high volatility generates uncertainty, affecting returns and further amplifying volatility [@french1987expected]. Most consequentially, volatility exhibits regime dependence with fundamentally different behavior during calm versus turbulent periods.

@hamilton1989new introduced regime-switching models where an unobserved Markov chain governs parameter changes. Applications to volatility produced Markov-Switching GARCH (MS-GARCH) models [@hamilton1994autoregressive; @gray1996modeling; @haas2004new]. While MS-GARCH captures distinct regimes, it imposes abrupt transitions and requires probabilistic regime inference. @klaassen2002improving addressed the path-dependence problem in MS-GARCH through expected regime-conditional variances, though computational complexity remains substantial.

@terasvirta1994specification developed the Smooth Transition Autoregressive (STAR) framework allowing continuous, gradual transitions:
$$
y_t = (1 - G(s_t)) \Phi_1(y_{t-r}) + G(s_t) \Phi_2(y_{t-r}) + \epsilon_t
$$
where $G(\cdot)$ is a bounded transition function and $s_t$ is the transition variable. The STAR framework nests both linear models (as $\gamma \to 0$) and threshold autoregressions (as $\gamma \to \infty$). @granger1993modelling provided comprehensive guidance on specification, estimation, and evaluation of nonlinear time series models within this framework.

@mcaleer2008realized extended the HAR framework with smooth transition dynamics, proposing the Multiple-Regime Smooth Transition HAR (HARST) model. Applied to 16 DJIA stocks using high-frequency TAQ data, they demonstrated that logistic transition functions driven by lagged returns capture both long memory (through the HAR structure) and sign/size asymmetries (through regime-dependent coefficients). Their analysis revealed that regime transitions typically occur at large negative returns—around -3% to -6%—rather than at zero, with two regimes sufficient for most assets. Importantly, they found that combining HAR and HARST forecasts outperforms either model alone, suggesting complementary information content. However, this approach assumes regime transitions are identifiable from observable past data—an assumption violated when regime shifts are driven by unobserved factors including liquidity conditions, institutional positioning, and investor sentiment. The present paper addresses this limitation by inferring latent transition variables through deep state-space models.

## 2.3. Deep Probabilistic Models

The Variational Autoencoder framework [@kingma2014auto] enabled principled deep generative modeling with probabilistic latent representations, tractable training via Evidence Lower Bound maximization, and uncertainty quantification through posterior inference. These advances opened new possibilities for financial applications requiring both flexibility and interpretable uncertainty estimates.

Sequential extensions established deep probabilistic state-space models as a mature methodology. The Variational Recurrent Neural Network [@chung2015recurrent] incorporates latent variables at each time step, maintaining separation between latent dynamics and observations that mirrors classical state-space econometrics [@durbin2012time] while leveraging neural parameterization. The Deep Kalman Filter [@krishnan2017structured] provides an alternative architecture more explicitly parameterizing state-space dynamics. @rangapuram2018deep demonstrated the effectiveness of deep state-space models for probabilistic time series forecasting across diverse domains.

Machine learning approaches to financial prediction have achieved notable empirical success. @gu2020empirical provided comprehensive evidence that machine learning methods substantially improve cross-sectional return prediction relative to traditional factor models. Recent advances include DeepVol [@morenopino2024deepvol], which employs dilated causal convolutions for volatility forecasting from high-frequency data, achieving state-of-the-art performance but remaining a pure prediction framework without explicit regime structure. @hu2025novel proposed HAR-type models using graph neural networks to capture cross-asset dependencies, exploiting information spillovers across markets. @zhang2025forecasting extend HAR models with graph neural networks to capture nonlinear volatility spillovers across assets, demonstrating that nonlinear cross-asset dependencies—particularly when models are trained with quasi-likelihood loss—substantially improve short-horizon forecasts.

Despite substantial advances, no prior work integrates deep state-space models with interpretable econometric frameworks while using inferred latent states as transition variables. This methodological gap motivates the present paper's contribution. Furthermore, the deep learning volatility literature has uniformly failed to apply valid elicitability-based evaluation to tail risk forecasts, limiting the reliability of comparative claims.

## 2.4. Transformer Architectures for Time Series

The Transformer architecture [@vaswani2017attention], originally developed for natural language processing, has been adapted extensively for time series forecasting since 2021. These attention-based models offer an alternative paradigm to recurrent architectures, with several innovations addressing the specific challenges of temporal prediction.

@zhou2021informer introduced the Informer architecture, which addresses the quadratic complexity of standard self-attention through ProbSparse attention, reducing computational cost from $O(L^2)$ to $O(L \log L)$ for sequence length $L$. Designed for Long Sequence Time Series Forecasting (LSTF), Informer demonstrated substantial improvements on electricity, weather, and traffic datasets. However, the architecture lacks explicit latent state modeling, treating temporal dynamics implicitly through attention patterns rather than through structured state representations.

@lim2021temporal developed the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT), which combines LSTM layers for local processing with interpretable multi-head attention for long-range dependencies. TFT's architecture includes variable selection networks that provide instance-wise feature importance, offering interpretability advantages over black-box alternatives. Notably, their evaluation on the Oxford-Man Institute realized volatility dataset—the same data source used in the present study—showed modest improvements of 7-8% over baselines, substantially lower than the 20%+ gains observed on other domains. The authors found that attention weights on volatility data exhibited no persistent temporal patterns, instead acting as uniform smoothing filters, suggesting that standard Transformer architectures may not optimally capture volatility dynamics.

More recently, @nie2023patchtst proposed PatchTST, which applies the patching strategy from vision Transformers to time series. By treating contiguous subsequences as tokens rather than individual time points, PatchTST achieves state-of-the-art performance while maintaining computational efficiency. The channel-independence design, where each variable is processed separately, reduces model complexity but sacrifices cross-variable information that may be valuable for volatility forecasting.

While Transformer architectures have achieved remarkable success in general time series forecasting, their application to volatility presents specific challenges. Volatility exhibits regime-dependent dynamics, leverage effects, and jump clustering that may not be naturally captured by position-independent attention mechanisms. The present paper's VRNN-based approach offers complementary advantages: explicit latent state modeling enables regime identification, the recurrent structure naturally captures volatility persistence, and the smooth transition regression framework provides interpretable coefficient dynamics. Furthermore, the probabilistic state-space formulation yields uncertainty estimates essential for risk management applications—a feature absent from deterministic Transformer forecasts.

## 2.5. Tail Risk and Elicitability

Under Basel III/IV, Expected Shortfall has replaced Value-at-Risk as the standard market risk measure [@basel2019minimum]. However, while VaR is elicitable via the quantile loss function, ES is not individually elicitable [@gneiting2011making]—no scoring function exists for which ES alone minimizes expected loss. This has profound implications: any ES comparison using RMSE, MAE, or ad-hoc metrics is formally invalid. @acerbi2002coherence established the theoretical properties of ES as a coherent risk measure, though practical evaluation remained problematic until recent advances.

@fissler2016higher resolved this crisis by proving that the pair $(VaR_\alpha, ES_\alpha)$ is jointly elicitable. The FZ0 loss function provides the only theoretically valid framework for comparing ES forecasts:
$$
S_{FZ0}(Y; v, e) = -\ln(-e) - \frac{v}{e} + \left(1 + \frac{1}{e}\right)(Y - v) \mathbf{1}_{\{Y < v\}}
$$
@patton2019dynamic developed practical implementations and established asymptotic theory for elicitability-based model comparison in risk management applications.

The methodological deficiency in deep-learning volatility research is severe. Studies by @caporale2019modelling, @kim2018forecasting, and numerous others report ES comparisons without employing joint elicitability, rendering their comparative claims formally unfounded. The present paper addresses this gap by implementing rigorous FZ0-based evaluation, providing, to our knowledge, the first valid comparison of deep learning methods for Expected Shortfall forecasting in the realized volatility literature.
