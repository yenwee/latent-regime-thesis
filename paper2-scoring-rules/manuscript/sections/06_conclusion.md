# 6. Conclusion

This paper has developed a theoretical and empirical framework for regime-conditional evaluation of volatility forecasts. The central finding is that unconditional model rankings --- the standard in the volatility forecasting literature --- disagree with regime-conditional rankings for a substantial fraction of empirical forecasting comparisons. This disagreement is a structural consequence of the interaction between regime imbalance and regime-dependent model performance, formalized in Theorem 1 and confirmed in both simulation and empirical analysis.

The theoretical framework established five results. Proposition 1 showed that strictly consistent scoring functions retain their desirable properties under regime conditioning. Theorem 1 derived the necessary and sufficient conditions for ranking reversals, demonstrating that regime imbalance systematically overweights calm-period performance in unconditional aggregation. Theorem 2 identified conditions under which unconditional evaluation remains reliable, yielding a practical diagnostic. The extensions to the Model Confidence Set (Proposition 2) and to the Fissler-Ziegel loss for VaR and ES (Proposition 3) showed that regime conditioning applies to the full toolkit of modern forecast evaluation. Proposition 4 established asymptotic validity when the regime state is estimated from the data, accommodating both parametric and deep learning regime classifiers.

The simulation study confirmed these theoretical predictions quantitatively. Under calibrated Markov-switching and smooth transition data-generating processes with 200 Monte Carlo replications per configuration, pairwise misranking rates between STR and HAR models range from 2 to 68 percent under the MS DGP depending on regime imbalance, volatility contrast, and sample size, and average 48.1 percent across all STR DGP configurations. The misranking rate exceeds 50 percent at extreme regime imbalance ($\pi_L = 0.90$). Under a null DGP with no regime structure, the pairwise misranking rate is exactly zero.

The empirical application to a 21-asset panel spanning equities, fixed income, currencies, and commodities revealed ranking reversals in 58.7 percent of asset-horizon combinations (37 of 63), with the highest rate at the daily horizon (76.2 percent). The predominant reversal pattern involves STR models winning unconditionally while other models --- particularly STR-OBS, EGARCH-t, or HAR --- perform better in the low-volatility regime. The regime-conditional Model Confidence Set reveals a taxonomy of model specialization: the HAR model's unconditional MCS inclusion rate of 40 percent rises to 70 percent in the low-volatility regime, while the STR-SSM model's inclusion rate of 94 percent reaches 97 percent in the high-volatility regime. These distinctions, partially visible to unconditional evaluation, are consequential for model deployment: regime-conditional risk evaluation reveals that model performance varies substantially across volatility states.

Our recommendation to practitioners is direct: regime-switching volatility models should always be evaluated conditionally on regime state, in addition to the standard unconditional evaluation. The cost of regime-conditional evaluation is modest --- it requires only a regime classification and subsample analysis --- while the benefit is a more complete picture of model performance across the market states that the models were designed to address. When unconditional and conditional rankings agree, the analyst gains confidence in the unconditional result. When they disagree, the analyst gains information that is essential for application-specific model selection.

Several directions for future work emerge from this analysis. First, extending the framework to multivariate forecasts, where the object of interest is a covariance matrix rather than a scalar variance, would address the growing interest in portfolio-level volatility modeling. The elicitability of covariance matrix functionals remains an active research area, and regime-conditional extensions would be relevant for systemic risk applications. Second, incorporating time-varying regime definitions, in which the boundary between calm and stress evolves with market conditions, would address the critique that a fixed regime classification may not capture structural changes in volatility dynamics. Third, developing regime-conditional evaluation for density forecasts, building on the proper scoring rules for distributional forecasts of @gneiting2007strictly, would extend the framework beyond point forecasts and risk measures to the full predictive distribution. Fourth, extending the analysis to multi-period loss horizons, where the evaluation criterion aggregates losses over forecast horizons that may span multiple regime transitions, would formalize the attenuation effects documented at longer horizons and provide guidance on horizon-appropriate evaluation strategies. Fifth, our theoretical framework is restricted to two-regime systems, which, while standard in the Markov-switching volatility literature, may oversimplify markets that exhibit more than two distinct states (e.g., calm, moderate stress, and crisis). Extending the ranking reversal conditions to $K > 2$ regimes --- building on the multi-regime structure of Corollary 1 --- is a natural generalization that could accommodate richer state dynamics while preserving the core insight that unconditional aggregation can obscure regime-specific performance.
