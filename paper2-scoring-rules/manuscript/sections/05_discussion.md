# 5. Discussion

The theoretical and empirical results of this paper carry implications for how volatility forecasting models are evaluated, selected, and deployed. This section discusses the practical consequences, considers the overfitting objection, addresses multi-step-ahead complications, places the results within the broader literature, and identifies limitations of the framework.

## 5.1 Practical Implications for Model Selection

The central practical finding is that standard unconditional evaluation, while statistically valid in the sense that proper scoring rules remain strictly consistent [@gneiting2011making; @patton2011volatility], can produce misleading model rankings when the models under comparison are designed to handle different market states. The 58.7 percent reversal rate documented across our 21-asset panel is not a curiosity confined to unusual assets or extreme parameter configurations. It arises from the interaction of regime imbalance and regime-dependent model performance, with the predominant mechanism being that STR models win unconditionally but lose to other models in the low-volatility regime.

It is important to be precise about the nature of this finding. Unconditional evaluation is not biased in the statistical sense: it consistently estimates the unconditional expected loss, and the scoring rules we employ retain strict consistency regardless of the evaluation horizon. The issue is that the unconditional aggregation produces a summary that is misleading under regime imbalance --- it assigns weights to regime-specific performance in proportion to regime frequency rather than in proportion to economic importance. In applications where stress-period performance matters disproportionately (risk management, financial stability monitoring), this weighting is misaligned with the decision maker's objectives.

For practitioners, this finding suggests a straightforward modification to standard evaluation protocols. Rather than ranking models by average loss alone, analysts should supplement unconditional rankings with regime-conditional rankings and, where feasible, report the regime-conditional Model Confidence Set [@hansen2011model]. When regime-conditional and unconditional rankings agree, the analyst can deploy the unconditional winner with confidence. When they disagree, the analyst faces a decision that depends on the application's loss function: a risk manager who weights stress-period performance heavily should prefer the conditional winner in the high-volatility regime; a portfolio optimizer concerned with average forecast accuracy may reasonably prefer the unconditional winner. The diagnostic ratio $R$ defined in Remark 1 of Section 2.4 provides a quick assessment of whether regime-conditional evaluation is likely to produce different conclusions from unconditional evaluation.

The regime-conditional MCS analysis adds nuance to this recommendation. Rather than selecting a single best model, the MCS identifies a set of models that cannot be statistically distinguished at a given significance level. Our results show that this set changes composition across regimes. The HAR model, which is excluded from the unconditional MCS for 60 percent of asset-horizon pairs, enters the calm-regime MCS for 70 percent. The STR-SSM model, which is in the unconditional MCS for 94 percent, reaches 97 percent in the stress-regime MCS. A practitioner who evaluates models only unconditionally would dismiss the HAR model for most assets, missing the fact that it belongs to the set of statistically indistinguishable models during calm periods, where its simplicity, transparency, and computational efficiency may be preferred on grounds beyond forecast accuracy.

## 5.2 When Does Regime Complexity Pay?

A recurring question in the volatility modeling literature is whether the added complexity of regime-switching specifications is justified by improved forecast performance. Our framework provides a precise answer: it depends on the evaluation criterion. If the criterion is unconditional average loss, then the answer is equivocal. The STR-SSM model wins unconditionally in our panel, but by a modest margin, and the simpler HAR model is not significantly worse for many assets. If the criterion is loss during specific regimes, then regime complexity pays substantially during stress but imposes a cost during calm, and the net assessment depends on how the analyst weights the two regimes.

This formulation reframes the complexity question from "Is model A better than model B?" to "In which states is model A better than model B, and how much does the analyst care about each state?" The latter question is inherently application-dependent. A central bank conducting financial stability monitoring should weight stress-period performance heavily, as the purpose of the exercise is to assess vulnerabilities that manifest during stress. A proprietary trading desk concerned with daily position sizing may weight calm-period performance more heavily, as calm periods constitute the majority of trading days. By making the regime dependence of model performance explicit, regime-conditional evaluation allows each analyst to apply the weighting appropriate to their application, rather than accepting the implicit weighting imposed by unconditional averaging.

An important objection to this line of reasoning is that the ranking reversals we document may reflect correct penalization of overfitting rather than a limitation of unconditional evaluation. The argument is that regime-switching models overfit to in-sample noise, and their poor calm-period performance is the cost of this overfitting, which unconditional evaluation rightly penalizes. However, the pattern of results in our panel is inconsistent with an overfitting explanation. If the regime model were overfitting, it would perform poorly in *both* regimes out of sample, not just one. A model that memorizes in-sample patterns without learning genuine structure will generalize poorly regardless of the prevailing regime state. What we observe instead is a model that wins convincingly in the stress regime (included in the high-regime MCS for 97 percent of asset-horizon pairs) while being displaced by other models in the calm regime (90 percent low-regime inclusion). This asymmetric pattern --- gains where regime structure is present, costs where it is absent --- is the signature of a model that captures genuine regime-dependent dynamics rather than one that overfits. Moreover, the simulation results of Section 3 demonstrate that ranking reversals arise from the population-level interaction of regime imbalance and loss differential heterogeneity, not from overfitting: reversals persist as the sample size grows and increase in frequency with regime imbalance, neither of which is consistent with the overfitting hypothesis.

## 5.3 Multi-Step-Ahead Complications

The empirical analysis reveals that ranking reversals are most frequent at the daily horizon ($h = 1$, 76.2 percent) and lower at $h = 5$ (38.1 percent), though they increase again at $h = 22$ (61.9 percent). Multi-step-ahead forecasts introduce a conceptual complication for regime-conditional evaluation. The regime state at the forecast origin ($G_t$) need not persist through the forecast target ($G_{t+h}$). For $h = 22$, the probability of at least one regime transition during the forecast horizon is non-trivial: under the baseline Markov-switching calibration, the probability that the regime at $t + 22$ differs from the regime at $t$ exceeds 0.35. This means that conditioning on $G_t$ at the forecast origin does not guarantee that the entire forecast horizon is governed by a single regime. The forecast loss at $t + h$ reflects an average over possible regime paths between $t$ and $t + h$, and the regime-conditional evaluation based on $G_t$ is an approximation that becomes less precise as $h$ increases.

This complication does not invalidate regime-conditional evaluation at longer horizons, but it modifies its discriminating power. The non-monotonic reversal rates across horizons (76.2 percent at $h = 1$, 38.1 percent at $h = 5$, 61.9 percent at $h = 22$) suggest that temporal aggregation has a complex interaction with regime persistence: the weekly horizon may provide sufficient averaging to reduce reversals, while the monthly horizon preserves regime-conditional differences because regime states are sufficiently persistent that many 22-day windows fall entirely within one regime. Future work on regime-conditional evaluation at multi-step horizons could condition on the fraction of the forecast horizon spent in each regime, though this requires a model for regime dynamics that may itself introduce specification uncertainty.

## 5.4 Connection to the Broader Literature

The regime-conditional evaluation framework developed here connects to and extends several streams of research. The conditional predictive ability tests of @giacomini2006tests provide the closest antecedent: our regime-conditional comparison is a special case of their framework when the conditioning instrument is the regime indicator $h_t = \mathbf{1}(\hat{G}_t = g)$. The contribution of our analysis is to (i) characterize the specific conditions under which the unconditional test fails to detect regime-dependent differences in predictive ability, (ii) extend the framework to latent regime states via Proposition 4, and (iii) provide the MCS and risk evaluation extensions that are absent from the Giacomini-White framework. The bootstrap inference strategy follows @goncalves2004bootstrapping in accounting for the joint uncertainty in the two-stage procedure (regime estimation and forecast comparison), and the emphasis on proper scoring rules draws on @gneiting2011making and @patton2011volatility.

Recent work on regime-dependent evaluation has examined related phenomena from different angles. The volatility forecasting comparison of @patton2011volatility established that QLIKE-based rankings are robust to noise in the volatility proxy but did not address the regime-conditional dimension. The conditional autoregressive Value-at-Risk literature, including the CAViaR framework, has explored conditional risk evaluation but typically conditions on observable market variables rather than latent regime states. Our contribution is to formalize the regime-conditional dimension and demonstrate its quantitative importance for a broad panel of assets.

The results also have implications for an earlier empirical investigation of latent regime identification for volatility forecasting [@lim2026latent]. That analysis evaluated the STR-SSM model against benchmarks using unconditional QLIKE and the unconditional Model Confidence Set. The present findings suggest that those unconditional evaluations, while valid as far as they go, understated the STR-SSM model's advantage in the regime where it was specifically designed to improve: the high-volatility state. The regime-conditional analysis shows that the latent regime indicator from the VRNN does not merely provide a marginal improvement in average forecast accuracy; it identifies a state variable that partitions the forecast evaluation into regions where the model's advantage is concentrated and regions where it is absent. This is precisely the behavior one would expect from a well-specified regime model, and it provides additional evidence for the value of the latent state-space approach.

A separate investigation of whether latent regimes align with independent indicators of financial stress [@lim2026validation] can draw on the regime-conditional evaluation framework as a methodological tool. If the latent regimes identified by the VRNN are economically meaningful, then regime-conditional evaluation should reveal patterns that are consistent with external stress indicators. Specifically, the high-volatility regime identified by the VRNN should coincide with periods when the VIX is elevated, credit spreads are wide, or market liquidity is impaired. The regime-conditional MCS provides a formal framework for testing whether model performance varies systematically with these external indicators, independently of the VRNN's regime classification. A finding that regime-conditional model rankings are similar whether we condition on the VRNN's latent regime or on an observable stress indicator would provide strong validation that the latent regime captures economically meaningful variation in market conditions.

## 5.5 Limitations

Several limitations qualify the results of this paper. First, regime-conditional evaluation requires a regime classification, and any classification introduces the possibility of misspecification. When the regime indicator is estimated from the data, as in the VRNN approach, the classification is subject to estimation error, and the regime-conditional loss estimates inherit this uncertainty. Proposition 4 establishes asymptotic validity under consistent regime estimation, but we acknowledge that the VRNN does not provide classical asymptotic guarantees of convergence to a "true" latent state---the generated regressor problem is inherent to any deep-learning-derived conditioning variable whose asymptotic distribution is unknown. We do not claim that the VRNN regime indicator converges to the true data-generating state in a frequentist sense. Rather, our inferential framework rests on two pillars: the two-stage block bootstrap, which provides finite-sample validity by jointly resampling regime estimates and forecast errors, and the ex-post quantile proxy, which is entirely model-free and produces qualitatively similar results (reversal rate of 49.2% versus 58.7% with the VRNN indicator). The similarity of findings across these two fundamentally different regime definitions---one estimated by a neural network, the other defined by observable quantiles with no estimation error---provides the strongest evidence that our results are not artifacts of any particular regime estimation procedure.

Second, the regime classification discards observations in the interquartile range of the regime indicator, using only the bottom quartile (low regime) and top quartile (high regime) for conditional evaluation. This approach provides a cleaner separation between regimes but reduces the effective sample size by half. The VRNN produces a continuous regime indicator $q_t \in [0, 1]$, and a more refined analysis could use the continuous index directly in weighted evaluation, though this would complicate the MCS extension. The quartile-based classification has the virtue of robustness and direct connection to the two-regime theoretical framework, but it should be viewed as a first-order approximation.

Third, the regime-conditional evaluation framework, as developed here, treats the regime indicator as exogenous to the forecasting models. In practice, the VRNN-based regime indicator is estimated jointly with the STR-SSM model's forecasting parameters, creating a potential circularity: the model that generated the regime indicator is also being evaluated conditional on that indicator. We mitigate this concern by using the ex-post quantile proxy as an independent validation, and the similarity of results across the two regime definitions suggests that the circularity does not drive the main findings. Nevertheless, a fully satisfactory treatment would require regime indicators that are entirely external to any of the models being compared.

Fourth, the power of regime-conditional tests is limited by the effective sample size in each regime. The quartile-based classification assigns approximately 25 percent of observations to each of the low and high regimes, providing adequate power for most tests, but for assets with particularly calm histories the stress subsample may be too small for reliable inference. This limitation is intrinsic to the data rather than to the methodology, and it underscores the importance of long evaluation samples for regime-conditional analysis.
