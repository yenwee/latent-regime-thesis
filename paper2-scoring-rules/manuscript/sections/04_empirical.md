# 4. Empirical Application

This section applies the regime-conditional evaluation framework to the volatility forecasting results of @lim2026latent, which compared six competing models across a panel of 21 assets at three forecast horizons. The analysis reveals that unconditional QLIKE rankings produce ranking reversals for a majority of asset-horizon combinations, that the Model Confidence Set changes composition substantially when conditioned on regime state, and that risk evaluation metrics exhibit even more pronounced regime dependence than point forecast scores.

## 4.1 Data and Models

The data and model outputs are taken from the empirical application of @lim2026latent. The panel comprises 21 assets spanning five classes: equity indices (S&P 500, DJIA, NASDAQ-100, Russell 2000), sector ETFs (XLF, XLK, XLE, XLU), fixed income (IRX, TNX, TYX, IEF, TLT), major currency pairs (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, AUD/USD), and commodities (gold, copper, crude oil, natural gas). This cross-section provides variation in volatility dynamics, regime frequency, and the relative advantage of regime-switching models. The sample period runs from 2015 through 2025, with the out-of-sample evaluation period spanning approximately 2017 to 2025 (approximately 2,100 trading days per asset after accounting for the initial training window). Realized variance is computed from daily data using the Garman-Klass estimator [@garman1980estimation], and forecast evaluation uses a rolling window of 600 observations with segment-based VRNN retraining as described in @lim2026latent.

Six forecasting models are compared at horizons $h \in \{1, 5, 22\}$ days. The HAR model of @corsi2009simple provides the linear benchmark, using daily, weekly, and monthly averages of log realized variance as predictors. Two smooth transition HAR (STR-HAR) specifications augment the HAR with regime-dependent coefficients: STR-OBS uses an observable transition variable (the ratio of short-term to long-term realized volatility), while STR-SSM uses the latent regime indicator from a variational recurrent neural network (VRNN) trained on the same asset's return and volatility series, as developed in @lim2026latent. The GARCH(1,1) model of @bollerslev1986generalized, the EGARCH(1,1) model of @nelson1991conditional, and the Markov-switching GARCH (MS-GARCH) model of @haas2004new complete the comparison set. The model set is deliberately heterogeneous, including both regime-aware specifications (STR-OBS, STR-SSM, MS-GARCH) and regime-agnostic specifications (HAR, GARCH, EGARCH), to provide a comprehensive test of whether unconditional evaluation systematically disadvantages regime-switching models.

The regime conditioning variable for the evaluation framework is the VRNN-inferred regime indicator $q_t$ from @lim2026latent. We classify each out-of-sample observation into the low-volatility regime ($G_t = L$) when $q_t$ falls below its 25th percentile and the high-volatility regime ($G_t = H$) when $q_t$ exceeds its 75th percentile. Observations in the interquartile range are excluded from regime-conditional evaluation, providing a cleaner separation between distinct volatility states at the cost of reducing the effective sample size for conditional analysis. This quartile-based classification ensures that regime-conditional evaluations contrast genuinely different volatility environments rather than observations near the regime boundary.

## 4.2 Unconditional Versus Conditional Rankings

Table 2 reports unconditional QLIKE rankings for the six models across all 21 assets and three horizons. The results are broadly consistent with the findings of @lim2026latent: the STR-SSM model achieves the best average rank (1.83 across all 63 asset-horizon pairs), followed by STR-OBS (2.05), HAR (3.19), EGARCH-t (4.05), GARCH-t (4.13), and MS-GARCH-t (5.76). At $h = 1$, STR-SSM wins for 12 of 21 assets unconditionally, while STR-OBS wins for 4, GARCH-t for 3, and EGARCH-t for 2. The HAR model does not achieve the best unconditional QLIKE for any asset at $h = 1$. The MS-GARCH model is most frequently ranked last, reflecting its tendency toward parameter instability in the rolling-window estimation framework.

Tables 3 and 4 present the regime-conditional QLIKE rankings for the low-volatility and high-volatility regimes, respectively. The contrast with the unconditional rankings is informative. In the low-volatility regime (Table 3), the STR-OBS model achieves the best average rank (2.00 across all asset-horizon pairs), displacing STR-SSM (2.19) from the top position it holds unconditionally. The HAR model's average low-regime rank is 3.00, a modest improvement from its unconditional rank of 3.19. At $h = 1$, STR-OBS wins in the low regime for 11 of 21 assets, followed by EGARCH-t (5 assets), STR-SSM and GARCH-t (2 each), and HAR (1). The EGARCH model performs well in the low regime, where volatility dynamics are more regular and leverage effects provide forecasting value.

In the high-volatility regime (Table 4), the STR-SSM model's dominance is reinforced. It achieves the best conditional rank for 13 of 21 assets at $h = 1$ and an average rank of 1.60 across all asset-horizon pairs. The STR-OBS model has an average high-regime rank of 2.30, while the HAR model's average rank worsens to 3.25. GARCH-t (average rank 3.95) performs better than EGARCH-t (4.16) in the high regime, a reversal from the low regime. The divergence in model performance across regimes is consistent with the mechanism identified in Theorem 1: models that are adequate in calm periods may be inferior in stress periods, and the regime imbalance in unconditional evaluation can obscure these differences.

## 4.3 Ranking Reversals

A ranking reversal, as defined in Definition 3, occurs when the model ranked first unconditionally is not ranked first in at least one regime. Across the 63 asset-horizon pairs, ranking reversals occur in 37 cases (58.7 percent). This is a substantively high rate, indicating that unconditional QLIKE evaluation produces a misleading picture of relative model performance for a majority of the forecasting exercises in our panel.

The incidence of reversals varies systematically across horizons and asset classes. Reversals are most frequent at the daily horizon ($h = 1$), where 76.2 percent of the 21 assets (16 of 21) exhibit a reversal. This is consistent with the theoretical prediction that reversals are more likely when regime-conditional loss differentials are large relative to unconditional differentials, and daily forecasts exhibit the sharpest differentiation between calm and stress periods because the regime-switching dynamics operate at the daily frequency. At $h = 5$, the reversal rate drops to 38.1 percent (8 of 21). At $h = 22$, the reversal rate is 61.9 percent (13 of 21), higher than the weekly horizon. The non-monotonic pattern across horizons suggests that temporal aggregation does not uniformly attenuate regime-conditional differences; at the monthly horizon, the persistence of regime states means that many 22-day forecast windows fall entirely within a single regime, preserving the regime-conditional differences that drive reversals.

Across asset classes, fixed-income assets exhibit the highest reversal rate at 73.3 percent of asset-horizon combinations (11 of 15). This reflects the distinctive volatility dynamics of interest rate markets, where the regime contrast between calm and stress volatility is sharp. Commodities show the second-highest reversal rate at 66.7 percent (8 of 12), followed by equity sectors at 58.3 percent (7 of 12), equity indices at 50.0 percent (6 of 12), and currencies at 41.7 percent (5 of 12).

The direction of reversals reveals an important pattern: in 91.9 percent of reversals (34 of 37), the unconditional winner is an STR model (either STR-SSM or STR-OBS), while the conditional winner in the low-volatility regime is a different model. Only 8.1 percent of reversals (3 of 37) have a GARCH-family model as the unconditional winner. This indicates that in our panel, the predominant reversal mechanism operates through the low-volatility regime: an STR model wins overall (benefiting from its stress-regime advantage), but a different model --- often STR-OBS, EGARCH-t, or HAR --- produces better forecasts specifically during calm periods. Of the 37 reversals, 78.4 percent (29 cases) involve a low-regime reversal (the conditional winner in the low regime differs from the unconditional winner), while 35.1 percent (13 cases) involve a high-regime reversal. This asymmetry is consistent with the mechanism identified in Theorem 1: the low-volatility regime contains the majority of observations and exhibits more diverse model performance, so regime conditioning is most likely to reveal differences in this well-populated regime.

Table 5 summarizes the reversal patterns by identifying, for each asset class and horizon, the fraction of pairs exhibiting reversals and the typical direction of the reversal. The predominant pattern is STR-SSM or STR-OBS winning unconditionally, with a different model (often STR-OBS or EGARCH-t in the low regime) taking the conditional lead during calm periods.

Figure 3 presents a heatmap of ranking reversals across the full panel. Each cell represents one asset-horizon pair, with color intensity indicating the magnitude of the rank change between unconditional and high-regime conditional evaluation. The heatmap reveals a clear pattern: rank changes are concentrated in the upper-left quadrant (short horizons, equity and fixed-income assets), where regime dynamics are most pronounced and regime imbalance is highest.

## 4.4 Regime-Conditional Model Confidence Set

The ranking reversals documented in Section 4.3 describe point estimates of model rankings. The regime-conditional Model Confidence Set, developed in Section 2.5, provides a formal inferential framework that accounts for estimation uncertainty. We construct the MCS at the 10 percent significance level ($\alpha = 0.10$) using the range statistic with HAC-consistent variance estimation, separately for each regime, each asset, and each horizon.

Table 6 reports the MCS inclusion rates --- the fraction of asset-horizon pairs for which each model is included in the MCS --- for the unconditional, low-regime conditional, and high-regime conditional settings. The results are pronounced. The STR-SSM model is included in the unconditional MCS for 94 percent of asset-horizon pairs, the highest rate among all models. However, this headline figure obscures a regime dependence: STR-SSM is included in the high-regime MCS for 97 percent of pairs but in the low-regime MCS for 90 percent. The model's unconditional MCS rate sits between these regime-specific rates.

The HAR model shows a different pattern. Its unconditional MCS inclusion rate is 40 percent, indicating that it is significantly outperformed by at least one other model in 60 percent of asset-horizon pairs. In the low-regime conditional MCS, the HAR model's inclusion rate rises to 70 percent --- notably higher than its unconditional rate. In the high-regime MCS, its inclusion rate is 65 percent. The HAR model thus benefits substantially from regime conditioning in the low regime, where its simple autoregressive structure captures the smooth dynamics of calm-period volatility. The horizon-level breakdown is revealing: at $h = 1$, the HAR model is included in the unconditional MCS for 71 percent of assets, in the low-regime MCS for 86 percent, and in the high-regime MCS for 86 percent. At $h = 5$, these rates diverge sharply: 24 percent unconditional, 67 percent low-regime, and 48 percent high-regime. At $h = 22$, the pattern is 24 percent unconditional, 57 percent low-regime, and 62 percent high-regime.

The STR-OBS model has an unconditional MCS inclusion rate of 75 percent, rising to 94 percent in the low regime and 79 percent in the high regime. The STR-OBS model's notably high low-regime inclusion rate (94 percent) suggests that the observable transition variable is particularly effective at capturing calm-period dynamics, even though STR-SSM achieves a higher high-regime inclusion rate (97 versus 79 percent). The GARCH-t model has an unconditional inclusion rate of 21 percent, rising to 25 percent in the low regime and 40 percent in the high regime. The EGARCH-t model shows a similar pattern (unconditional 21 percent, low regime 24 percent, high regime 41 percent), with the leverage effect's greater relevance during stress contributing to the improved high-regime rate. The MS-GARCH-t model, despite its explicit regime-switching structure, has the lowest unconditional MCS inclusion rate (8 percent) and improves only modestly in the high regime (27 percent), a finding attributable to the estimation challenges of fitting a full Markov-switching GARCH model in a rolling window with limited data.

Figure 4 illustrates the regime-conditional MCS composition as an Euler diagram. The diagram identifies models that belong to the calm-MCS only, the stress-MCS only, both, or neither, averaged across the panel. The HAR model falls predominantly in the calm-MCS-only region, the STR-SSM model falls predominantly in the both region, and the MS-GARCH model falls predominantly in the neither region. This visual summary reinforces the central finding: unconditional MCS evaluation conflates calm-period and stress-period performance into a single number that can be misleading when models specialize across regimes.

## 4.5 Risk Evaluation

The extension of the regime-conditional framework to risk measures, developed in Section 2.6, has particular practical relevance because risk management performance during stress is more consequential than during calm. We evaluate Value-at-Risk at the 1 percent level and Expected Shortfall at the 2.5 percent level, using the FZ0 loss of @fissler2016higher as the joint scoring function. VaR and ES forecasts are generated from each model's conditional variance forecast combined with a distributional assumption (Gaussian for HAR, STR-OBS, STR-SSM, and GARCH; Student-$t$ for EGARCH; regime-dependent mixture for MS-GARCH).

Table 7 reports the regime-conditional FZ0 loss, averaged across the panel, for each model in each regime. The FZ0 loss values exhibit substantial scale variation across regimes, reflecting the fundamentally different loss magnitudes during calm versus stress periods. At $h = 1$ with the 1 percent VaR level, the STR-SSM model achieves the lowest (most negative) FZ0 loss in the high regime ($-2.69$), closely followed by HAR ($-2.63$) and STR-OBS ($-2.43$). In the low regime, the STR-SSM model has the highest FZ0 loss among the STR and HAR models ($3.38 \times 10^8$, compared to $9.51 \times 10^7$ for HAR), indicating a cost of unnecessary regime-switching complexity during calm periods. The regime-conditional FZ0 analysis reveals that the relative performance of models differs substantially between regimes: models with flexible regime-dependent structures incur higher losses during calm periods (where their complexity is unnecessary) but achieve lower losses during stress periods (where their regime adaptation is valuable).

The VaR calibration results reinforce this pattern. During the high-volatility regime at $h = 1$, the HAR model's 1 percent VaR is violated 1.80 percent of the time on average across the panel --- nearly twice the nominal rate --- indicating undercoverage during stress. The STR-SSM model achieves a violation rate of 1.58 percent in the high regime, closer to the nominal level. In the low regime, the pattern reverses: the HAR model's violation rate is 2.13 percent and the STR-SSM model's is 2.75 percent, both well above the 1 percent nominal rate, indicating that the low-regime subsample (defined by the bottom quartile of the regime indicator) presents calibration challenges for all models. Unconditional violation rates are 1.72 percent for HAR and 2.02 percent for STR-SSM. At the 5 percent VaR level, the regime dependence is more pronounced: during the high regime at $h = 5$, the HAR model's violation rate is 7.82 percent while the STR-SSM model achieves 6.65 percent; during the low regime, the rates are 4.96 percent and 6.24 percent respectively, illustrating the reversal of relative model performance across regimes.

Figure 5 presents bar charts of the FZ0 loss by model and regime, illustrating the scale of the regime dependence. The visual representation makes clear that the models are essentially interchangeable during calm periods but differ substantially during stress, precisely the setting where regime-conditional evaluation adds the most value.

The economic significance of regime-conditional risk evaluation is apparent from the scale of regime-dependent FZ0 differences. The regime-conditional analysis reveals that model rankings for risk metrics are not stable across volatility states, reinforcing the central message that unconditional evaluation can produce misleading conclusions about the relative quality of risk forecasts during the stress episodes that are most consequential for portfolio management and regulatory compliance.

## 4.6 Robustness

Several robustness checks support the stability of the main findings. The empirical analysis uses a quartile-based regime classification (low regime when the VRNN indicator is below its 25th percentile, high regime when above its 75th percentile), which provides a cleaner separation between regimes at the cost of discarding observations near the regime boundary. The 58.7 percent reversal rate is robust to the specific choice of conditioning variable and threshold: the qualitative pattern of regime-dependent model rankings and the predominance of low-regime reversals are preserved under alternative specifications. The regime-conditional MCS results are also stable, with the key finding --- that model inclusion rates differ substantially between low-regime and high-regime evaluations --- holding across variations in the MCS significance level and bootstrap parameters.
