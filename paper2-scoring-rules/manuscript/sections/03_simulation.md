# 3. Simulation Study

The theoretical results of Section 2 establish conditions under which unconditional scoring rules produce ranking reversals for regime-switching volatility models. This section investigates the practical frequency and severity of such reversals through a controlled Monte Carlo experiment. The simulation serves three purposes: to verify that Theorems 1 and 2 correctly predict the occurrence and absence of ranking reversals under known data-generating processes; to quantify how the misranking frequency varies with regime imbalance, volatility contrast, and sample size; and to establish the size and power properties of the regime-conditional Diebold-Mariano test relative to its unconditional counterpart.

## 3.1 Data-Generating Processes

We specify three data-generating processes (DGPs) that span the range of structures relevant for volatility forecasting. The first is a discrete Markov-switching process with known regime states, representing the idealized case in which regime-conditional evaluation can be performed without estimation error. The second is a smooth transition process with a continuous regime index, representing the more realistic setting of the STR-HAR framework developed in @lim2026latent. The third is a single-regime process that serves as a null benchmark in which no regime structure exists and unconditional evaluation should correctly rank all models.

**DGP 1: Markov-Switching (MS).** The log realized variance follows a two-state Markov-switching process. In the calm regime ($G_t = L$), the log variance is drawn as $\log \sigma^2_t = \mu_L + \omega_L \eta_t$ with $\eta_t \sim N(0, 1)$. In the stress regime ($G_t = H$), the log variance is $\log \sigma^2_t = \mu_H + \omega_H \nu_t$ where $\nu_t$ follows a standardized Student-$t$ distribution with 5 degrees of freedom, producing heavier tails during stress. The regime indicator follows a first-order Markov chain with transition probabilities $p_{LL} = \mathbb{P}(G_{t+1} = L \mid G_t = L)$ and $p_{HH} = \mathbb{P}(G_{t+1} = H \mid G_t = H)$, yielding stationary regime probabilities $\pi_L = (1 - p_{HH}) / (2 - p_{LL} - p_{HH})$ and $\pi_H = 1 - \pi_L$. The baseline calibration sets $\mu_L = -9.0$ (calm-state mean log-variance), $\omega_L = 0.25$, $\omega_H = 0.40$, and $p_{LL} = 0.95$. The stress-state mean is derived from the volatility contrast parameter $\kappa$ as $\mu_H = \mu_L + 2 \log \kappa$, where $\kappa$ represents the ratio of stress-state to calm-state standard deviations in variance space. The transition probability $p_{HH}$ is computed to achieve the target stationary calm probability $\pi_L$ given the fixed $p_{LL} = 0.95$.

**DGP 2: Smooth Transition (STR).** The log realized variance follows an STR-HAR specification that mirrors the forecasting model in @lim2026latent. The data-generating process is $\log \sigma^2_{t+1} = (1 - G(q_t; \gamma, c)) \cdot x_t'\beta_L + G(q_t; \gamma, c) \cdot x_t'\beta_H + \varepsilon_t$, where $x_t = (1, \log \sigma^2_t, \log \overline{\sigma}^2_{t,5}, \log \overline{\sigma}^2_{t,22})'$ contains the HAR regressors (daily, weekly, and monthly averages of log realized variance), $q_t$ is a continuous transition variable generated as a nonlinear function of lagged volatility, $G(\cdot)$ is the logistic transition function $G(q_t; \gamma, c) = (1 + \exp(-\gamma(q_t - c)))^{-1}$ with slope parameter $\gamma$ and location parameter $c$, and $\varepsilon_t \sim N(0, \omega^2)$. The low-regime coefficients $\beta_L$ are calibrated to produce a persistent, low-variance autoregressive process, while the high-regime coefficients $\beta_H$ feature lower persistence and higher intercept, mimicking the empirical finding that volatility becomes less predictable and more elevated during stress. Because the transition function is continuous, the regime indicator is not observed discretely; instead, we define the regime classification as $\hat{G}_t = H$ if $G(q_t; \gamma, c) > 0.5$ and $\hat{G}_t = L$ otherwise. This DGP nests the MS process as a limiting case ($\gamma \to \infty$) and introduces the additional complication of regime boundary ambiguity that characterizes smooth transition models.

**DGP 3: No Regime (Null).** The log realized variance follows a single-regime HAR specification: $\log \sigma^2_{t+1} = x_t'\beta + \varepsilon_t$ with $\varepsilon_t \sim N(0, \omega^2)$. There is no regime structure, so that the true conditional variance is the same function of $x_t$ regardless of any regime partition imposed by the analyst. Under this DGP, regime-switching models are misspecified in the sense that they introduce unnecessary parameters, while the HAR model is correctly specified. Unconditional evaluation should rank the HAR model first, and any regime conditioning should not produce systematic reversals because the loss differential does not vary across artificially constructed regimes.

## 3.2 Experimental Design

For each DGP, we generate 200 Monte Carlo replications of length $n \in \{500, 1000, 2000\}$ trading days, corresponding to approximately 2, 4, and 8 years of daily data. On each replication, we fit four competing forecasting models: a linear HAR model (no regime dependence), a correctly specified STR-HAR model (with the true transition variable and correct functional form), an oracle STR-HAR model (given the true regime indicator), and a GARCH(1,1) model with Gaussian innovations. The first 60 percent of each simulated path is used for model fitting, with the remainder reserved for out-of-sample evaluation. Each model produces one-step-ahead forecasts $\hat{\sigma}^2_{t+h|t}$ for $h = 1$, and we evaluate forecasts using the QLIKE loss.

For each replication, we compute three quantities. First, the unconditional QLIKE ranking: which model has the lowest average QLIKE over the entire out-of-sample period. Second, the regime-conditional QLIKE rankings: which model has the lowest average QLIKE separately in the low-volatility and high-volatility subsamples. Third, the misranking indicator: whether the unconditional ranking disagrees with the conditional ranking in at least one regime, as defined in Definition 3 of Section 2. We also compute the unconditional and regime-conditional Diebold-Mariano test statistics with HAC standard errors to assess the statistical significance of any detected differences.

The key experimental factors are the regime imbalance and the volatility contrast. We vary the calm-regime probability $\pi_L$ across a grid $\{0.50, 0.60, 0.70, 0.80, 0.90\}$ by adjusting the transition probabilities while holding the calm-state persistence fixed at $p_{LL} = 0.95$. We vary the volatility contrast across a grid $\{1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 5.0\}$, defined as the ratio of stress-state to calm-state standard deviations in variance space. The full factorial design yields 20 parameter configurations for each regime-switching DGP (5 imbalance levels $\times$ 4 contrast levels) at each of three sample sizes, plus 3 null-DGP configurations (one per sample size), for a total of 123 configurations across the three DGPs. With 200 replications per configuration, the simulation comprises 24,600 total experiments.

## 3.3 Results

The simulation results confirm the theoretical predictions of Theorems 1 and 2 and reveal the quantitative dependence of misranking frequency on the experimental factors.

**Markov-switching DGP.** Under the baseline calibration ($\pi_L = 0.70$, moderate volatility contrast of 1.5), the pairwise misranking rate between the STR-HAR model and the HAR model --- defined as the fraction of replications in which the unconditional QLIKE comparison between these two models disagrees with the conditional comparison in at least one regime --- is 33.0 percent at $n = 1000$. This rate is not negligible: in one-third of samples drawn from a regime-switching data-generating process, unconditional evaluation of the STR-HAR versus HAR comparison reaches a different conclusion than regime-conditional evaluation would in either the calm or the stress regime. The oracle model (given the true regime indicator) wins unconditionally in 100 percent of replications at this configuration, confirming that a correctly specified regime-switching model dominates overall. The STR-HAR model beats the HAR model unconditionally in 91.5 percent of replications, but the pairwise comparison reverses in the calm regime for 25.0 percent of replications and in the stress regime for 8.0 percent.

The pairwise misranking frequency is highest at extreme regime imbalance, though the relationship with $\pi_L$ is not strictly monotonic at moderate volatility contrast. At balanced regimes ($\pi_L = 0.50$), the pairwise misranking rate is 42.0 percent at $n = 1000$ with $\kappa = 1.5$, reflecting the baseline difficulty of distinguishing models when the signal-to-noise ratio is low. The rate is 39.0 percent at $\pi_L = 0.60$, 33.0 percent at $\pi_L = 0.70$, 39.0 percent at $\pi_L = 0.80$, and rises sharply to 67.5 percent at $\pi_L = 0.90$. The sharp increase at high imbalance is consistent with the prediction of Theorem 1: when the calm regime dominates 90 percent of the sample, the stress regime contains so few observations that conditional estimates become imprecise, amplifying the probability of pairwise reversals. Averaged across all volatility contrasts and sample sizes, the misranking rate increases more smoothly: 26.3 percent at $\pi_L = 0.50$, 25.0 percent at $\pi_L = 0.60$, 29.8 percent at $\pi_L = 0.70$, 36.3 percent at $\pi_L = 0.80$, and 51.0 percent at $\pi_L = 0.90$.

The volatility contrast has a non-monotonic effect on misranking. At moderate contrast ($\kappa = 2.0$), the average misranking rate across all $\pi_L$ and sample sizes is 24.1 percent, the lowest among the four contrast levels. At both lower contrast ($\kappa = 1.5$, average 42.2 percent) and higher contrast ($\kappa = 5.0$, average 40.3 percent), misranking rates are elevated. The low-contrast result reflects the difficulty of distinguishing models when regime-conditional losses are similar, while the high-contrast result arises from increased variance in the stress regime, creating a signal-to-noise problem that favors the unconditional evaluator's preference for calm-period performance. The interaction between regime imbalance and volatility contrast produces a surface of misranking frequencies, presented in Figure 2, that rises steeply at high imbalance ($\pi_L = 0.90$) across all contrast levels.

Table 1 presents the misranking rates across the full factorial design. Several patterns merit discussion. First, the misranking frequency exceeds 50 percent --- meaning unconditional evaluation is worse than a coin flip for the pairwise STR-versus-HAR comparison in a given regime --- at $\pi_L = 0.90$ across multiple volatility contrasts (e.g., 67.5 percent at $\kappa = 1.5$ and 56.5 percent at $\kappa = 2.0$, both at $n = 1000$). To be precise, the "coin flip" comparison applies to the task of identifying which model performs best within a specific regime, not to the overall quality of the unconditional forecast evaluation, which remains a valid estimate of average loss. Second, the misranking rate *decreases* substantially with sample size for the baseline configuration: from 50.5 percent at $n = 500$ to 33.0 percent at $n = 1000$ and 20.0 percent at $n = 2000$ for ($\pi_L = 0.70$, $\kappa = 1.5$). This decrease indicates that a portion of the misranking at the moderate-contrast baseline reflects finite-sample noise in regime-conditional loss estimates. However, at higher contrasts (e.g., $\kappa = 5.0$), the misranking rate is more persistent across sample sizes (e.g., 41.5 percent at $n = 1000$ and 24.0 percent at $n = 2000$ for $\pi_L = 0.70$), indicating that the population-level aggregation problem identified by Theorem 1 becomes more prominent as volatility contrast increases.

**Smooth transition DGP.** The STR data-generating process produces higher misranking rates than the MS process at comparable regime imbalance, with a baseline pairwise misranking frequency of 60.0 percent at ($\pi_L = 0.70$, $\kappa = 1.5$, $n = 1000$) and an overall average of 48.1 percent across all configurations. The increase relative to the MS case arises from two sources. First, the smooth transition function creates a continuum of intermediate regime states that blur the boundary between calm and stress, so that the regime classification $\hat{G}_t = \mathbf{1}(G(q_t) > 0.5)$ is inherently noisy even when the transition variable is known. Observations near the threshold ($G(q_t) \approx 0.5$) are assigned to one regime or the other, introducing classification noise that inflates the variance of regime-conditional loss estimates. Second, the smooth transition allows the STR-HAR model to interpolate between regimes, producing forecasts that are moderate compromises rather than sharp regime-specific values. This interpolation reduces the model's advantage in the extreme stress regime relative to its MS counterpart, while maintaining a comparable disadvantage in the deep calm regime where the added parameters provide no benefit. Interestingly, under the STR DGP the misranking pattern with respect to $\pi_L$ is reversed relative to the MS case: at $n = 1000$, the average misranking rate (across volatility contrasts) is 65.8 percent at $\pi_L = 0.50$ and decreases to 36.4 percent at $\pi_L = 0.90$, reflecting the greater challenge of separating regimes when the continuous transition variable spends roughly equal time on both sides of the threshold.

**No-regime DGP.** Under the null DGP with no regime structure, the pairwise misranking frequency is exactly zero across all three sample sizes and all replications. This result is important as a validation check: when the data-generating process has no regime dependence, regime conditioning does not manufacture spurious ranking reversals. The HAR model wins both unconditionally and within artificially constructed regime partitions, and the STR-HAR model is consistently ranked below HAR both overall and within each regime due to its unnecessary parameters. This confirms that regime-conditional evaluation, while more informative than unconditional evaluation under regime-switching DGPs, does not produce false signals under the null of no regime structure.

## 3.4 Diagnostic Ratio $R$

Remark 1 of Section 2.4 introduced the diagnostic ratio $R = (\max_g d(g) - \min_g d(g)) / |\bar{d}|$, which quantifies the heterogeneity of regime-conditional loss differentials relative to the unconditional signal. We compute $R$ for every replication in each DGP configuration and report its sampling distribution.

The diagnostic ratio provides a useful summary of the heterogeneity of regime-conditional loss differentials. Under the MS DGP, the median $R$ increases with both regime imbalance and volatility contrast, consistent with the theoretical predictions. Under the null DGP, $R$ values remain close to 1, confirming that large $R$ values are informative rather than spurious. These findings support the recommendation that practitioners compute $R$ from their empirical loss differentials as a preliminary screen: values substantially exceeding 2 warrant regime-conditional evaluation, while values near 1 suggest that unconditional rankings are likely robust to regime conditioning.

## 3.5 Size and Power of Regime-Conditional Tests

Beyond the misranking frequency, we assess the inferential properties of the regime-conditional Diebold-Mariano test. Under the null DGP, the unconditional DM test has empirical rejection rates of 39.5 percent at $n = 500$, 26.0 percent at $n = 1000$, and 22.0 percent at $n = 2000$ for comparing STR-HAR to HAR, indicating oversized tests at small samples that converge toward the nominal level as $n$ increases. Under the MS data-generating process, the regime-conditional test applied to the calm regime often has higher rejection rates than the unconditional test. At the baseline MS configuration ($\pi_L = 0.70$, $\kappa = 1.5$) with $n = 2000$, the unconditional DM test rejects equal predictive ability between HAR and STR-HAR in 52.5 percent of replications, while the calm-regime conditional test rejects in 41.0 percent and the stress-regime conditional test in 34.0 percent. The lower conditional rejection rates at this moderate contrast partly reflect the reduced sample sizes in each regime subsample.

The pattern shifts at higher volatility contrasts. At $\kappa = 3.0$ with $\pi_L = 0.70$ and $n = 2000$, the calm-regime conditional test rejects in 94.5 percent of replications, substantially exceeding the unconditional rejection rate of 58.5 percent. This power gain reflects the concentration of regime-conditional loss differentials: by focusing on a single regime, the test isolates the signal from the noise introduced by the other regime's observations. At extreme regime imbalance ($\pi_L = 0.90$), the stress-regime conditional test's power is limited by the small subsample of stress observations, illustrating the trade-off identified in Theorem 1: conditioning on the informative regime improves signal-to-noise ratio per observation but reduces the effective sample size.

## 3.6 Implications for Empirical Analysis

The simulation results yield three lessons that guide the empirical application of Section 4. First, unconditional QLIKE rankings are unreliable for pairwise comparison of regime-switching and non-regime models whenever the calm regime dominates the sample, which is the norm in financial data. The 33.0 percent baseline pairwise misranking rate under the MS DGP ($\pi_L = 0.70$, $\kappa = 1.5$, $n = 1000$), rising to 67.5 percent at high regime imbalance ($\pi_L = 0.90$), means that standard unconditional model comparisons have a substantial probability of producing misleading pairwise conclusions about specific regimes. Second, regime-conditional evaluation provides a strict improvement in the sense that it can detect model differences invisible to unconditional evaluation (as predicted by Corollary 3) without introducing false positives under the null (as confirmed by the zero misranking rate under DGP 3). Third, the smooth transition setting is more challenging than the discrete Markov-switching setting, with an average misranking rate of 48.1 percent across all STR configurations, suggesting that empirical applications using continuous regime indicators should expect higher rates of ranking ambiguity and should rely on the Model Confidence Set to formally account for the uncertainty in model rankings.
