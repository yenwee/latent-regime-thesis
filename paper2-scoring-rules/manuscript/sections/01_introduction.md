# 1. Introduction

Regime-switching volatility models are evaluated almost exclusively by averaging a loss function over the entire out-of-sample period, yet the very premise of these models is that forecast dynamics are regime-dependent. This mismatch between conditional model structure and unconditional evaluation is the starting point of this paper. When evaluation is unconditional, every observation receives equal weight regardless of the prevailing regime. Because calm periods substantially outnumber stress episodes in typical financial samples --- a feature documented across equity, fixed-income, and currency markets [@hamilton1989new; @ang2002regime] --- average loss is dominated by performance during the tranquil state. A regime-switching model that delivers genuine improvements during stress, precisely the conditions it was designed for, may nonetheless be ranked below a simpler linear model that performs adequately during the calm periods that constitute the majority of the evaluation sample.

Standard practice follows naturally from the scoring-rule consistency results of @patton2011volatility, who proved that the QLIKE loss function correctly ranks volatility forecasts even when the true volatility is unobservable and only a noisy proxy is available. This is an important property, and our argument is not directed against it. Rather, we contend that the typical *application* of consistent scoring rules --- unconditional averaging over the full sample --- is incomplete when the data-generating process is regime-dependent. A scoring rule can be strictly consistent in the sense of @gneiting2011making and @nolde2017elicitability and yet, when averaged unconditionally over a regime-imbalanced sample, produce model rankings that obscure or even reverse the ordering that would emerge within individual regimes. We formalize this observation and establish the precise conditions under which such ranking reversals occur.

The conditional evaluation of forecasts has received attention in the literature. @diebold1995comparing introduced the foundational framework for comparing predictive accuracy, while @giacomini2006tests developed conditional predictive ability tests that condition on observable information sets. @hansen2005forecast and @hansen2011model proposed the Model Confidence Set for multiple model comparisons. Crucially, however, the Giacomini-White tests condition on *observable* information sets; no existing framework addresses the problem of conditioning on *latent* regime states --- precisely the setting that arises with modern deep learning approaches to regime identification. Models such as Markov-switching GARCH [@hamilton1989new; @haas2004new], smooth transition specifications [@terasvirta1994specification; @lundbergh2002evaluating], and deep latent state-space approaches including variational recurrent neural networks [@krishnan2017structured; @rangapuram2018deep] all infer regime states from data rather than observing them directly, making the question of latent regime conditioning both practically relevant and theoretically nontrivial.

Our theoretical framework builds on the elicitability theory of @gneiting2011making, the consistent loss functions for volatility of @patton2011volatility, and the joint elicitability results of @fissler2016higher for Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall. We extend these foundations by developing a regime-conditional evaluation framework that preserves the desirable properties of proper scoring rules while enabling analysts to assess model performance separately within each regime. The contributions of this paper are fivefold. First, we prove that strictly consistent scoring functions remain strictly consistent under regime conditioning (Proposition 1), so that regime-conditional evaluation inherits the theoretical guarantees of unconditional proper scoring rules. Second, we derive necessary and sufficient conditions for unconditional ranking reversals (Theorem 1), characterizing exactly when two models can be correctly ordered within each regime yet incorrectly ordered overall. Third, we identify sufficient conditions under which unconditional evaluation can be trusted to preserve regime-level rankings (Theorem 2), providing a practical diagnostic for analysts. Fourth, we extend the regime-conditional framework to the Model Confidence Set of @hansen2011model and to the Fissler-Ziegel loss for joint VaR/ES evaluation, showing that a regime-conditional Model Confidence Set can identify "stress specialists" --- models that belong to the superior set during high-volatility regimes but not during calm periods, a distinction that is invisible to unconditional evaluation. Fifth, we prove that regime-conditional tests using consistently estimated latent regimes are asymptotically valid under regularity conditions specified in Section 2, ensuring that the framework applies to the growing class of models that employ neural network-based regime inference.

This final contribution connects our framework to the practical challenge of latent regime estimation. For models in the Markov-switching or deep state-space families, the regime indicator is not observed but inferred. We show that the regime-conditional evaluation remains valid provided the regime estimator satisfies a total-misclassification condition, and we discuss the connection to the generated-regressor literature and to the conditional predictive ability tests of @giacomini2006tests. For risk management applications, unconditional backtesting of Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall systematically favors models that calibrate well during the tranquil periods that dominate the sample, potentially at the expense of models that provide better tail coverage during the stress episodes when risk management matters most.

Our empirical application leverages the volatility forecasting results of @lim2026latent, which evaluated six competing models --- HAR, STR-HAR with observable and latent transitions, GARCH(1,1), EGARCH(1,1), and Markov-switching GARCH --- across a panel of 21 assets spanning equity indices, sector ETFs, government rates, major currency pairs, and commodities, at forecast horizons of 1, 5, and 22 days. Applying our regime-conditional evaluation framework to this dataset, we find that ranking reversals occur for 59 percent of asset-horizon pairs. In the majority of reversals, smooth transition models win unconditionally but lose in the calm regime to simpler specifications, while their advantage is concentrated in stress episodes. These findings demonstrate that the choice of evaluation framework can influence which model is deployed, and that unconditional evaluation may lead to suboptimal selections in precisely the conditions when forecast accuracy matters most.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 develops the theoretical framework, including the regime-conditional proper scoring rules, the ranking reversal theorems, and extensions to the Model Confidence Set and risk evaluation. Section 3 presents a Monte Carlo simulation study that quantifies the frequency and magnitude of ranking reversals under controlled conditions; the simulations confirm that reversals arise regularly in calibrated settings where the regime-switching model is the true data-generating process. Section 4 applies the framework to our 21-asset empirical panel. Section 5 discusses the practical implications and limitations, and Section 6 concludes.
