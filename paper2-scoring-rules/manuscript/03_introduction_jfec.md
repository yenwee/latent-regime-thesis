# 1. Introduction

Volatility regimes are a central feature of financial markets. Periods of elevated uncertainty cluster together, persistence varies with market conditions, and the relationship between past and future volatility shifts across calm and turbulent states. A substantial literature has developed models that capture these regime dynamics, from Markov-switching GARCH [@hamilton1989new; @gray1996modeling; @haas2004new] to smooth transition specifications [@granger1993modelling; @terasvirta1994specification] and, more recently, deep state-space approaches that infer latent states from data [@chung2015recurrent; @krishnan2017structured].

A common feature of this literature is that regimes are treated as asset-specific. Each asset's volatility process is modeled independently, with its own regime indicator governing transitions between high and low volatility states. When cross-asset structure is introduced, it typically takes the form of variance transmission---spillovers that propagate shocks from one asset to another---rather than shared regime dynamics. The implicit assumption is that while volatility levels may be connected across assets, the underlying regime state is idiosyncratic.

This assumption is difficult to reconcile with the behavior of financial markets during systemic stress. The 2008 financial crisis, the COVID-19 market disruption, and successive monetary policy shocks affected volatility across asset classes simultaneously. These episodes suggest that regime transitions may contain a systemic component: a common factor that governs when markets shift from calm to turbulent states, even if the magnitude of that shift varies across assets.

This paper develops a framework that separates the timing of regime transitions from asset-specific volatility dynamics by introducing a shared latent regime state inferred from cross-asset volatility observations. We extend a deep state-space model to multivariate realized volatility data to infer a common latent state that governs regime transitions across assets. This shared latent state serves as the transition variable in asset-specific smooth transition HAR forecasting equations, allowing regime timing to be informed by cross-asset evidence while preserving heterogeneity in how individual assets respond to regime shifts. The resulting architecture implements partial pooling: regimes are synchronized across assets, but their impact on volatility dynamics is asset-specific.

The approach differs from existing multivariate volatility models in a fundamental way. Models in the Diebold-Yilmaz tradition [@diebold2009measuring; @diebold2012better] measure variance transmission: how a shock to one asset's volatility affects another's. Network models of volatility spillovers extend this to high-dimensional settings. These frameworks capture important dynamics, but they model the propagation of shocks within a regime, not the transition between regimes. A market-wide shift from calm to turbulent conditions is a different phenomenon from a volatility shock that propagates across assets.

Our model also differs from factor-based approaches that extract common volatility components. Dynamic factor models [@stock2002forecasting] and their volatility extensions identify latent factors that explain cross-sectional variation in volatility levels. These factors capture comovement in volatility magnitudes. In contrast, our shared latent state governs regime transitions---the timing of shifts between volatility states---not the level of volatility itself.

The distinction matters for forecasting. If regime transitions have a systemic component, then pooling information across assets should improve regime identification, particularly when the signal in any single asset is weak. An asset-specific model must identify regime shifts from its own volatility history alone. A shared regime model can exploit cross-asset evidence: if most assets show signs of transitioning to a high-volatility state, this informs the regime probability for assets where the signal is ambiguous.

We implement this idea by extending the Variational Recurrent Neural Network (VRNN) framework to multivariate observations. The encoder takes stacked realized volatility from all assets in the panel and produces a shared latent state. This latent state serves as the transition variable in asset-specific STR-HAR forecasting equations, allowing smooth transitions between low and high volatility regimes that are synchronized across assets but heterogeneous in magnitude.

We evaluate the framework on a panel of 21 assets spanning equities, fixed income, currencies, commodities, and cryptocurrencies using an out-of-sample forecasting design. The results show that shared latent regimes improve volatility forecasting for a subset of assets---those most sensitive to systemic stress conditions---while asset-specific regime models perform comparably for assets dominated by idiosyncratic dynamics. The gains from pooling are concentrated during periods of market transition, when regime identification is ambiguous and cross-asset information is most valuable.

We further show that the shared latent regime captures information beyond observable proxies such as the VIX or cross-asset average volatility, justifying the use of latent regime inference over observable transition variables. Conceptually, the paper distinguishes between variance transmission and regime transmission, highlighting that synchronized regime shifts represent a distinct phenomenon not captured by traditional multivariate volatility or spillover models.

The paper makes three contributions. First, we introduce a formal distinction between variance transmission and regime transmission in multivariate volatility modeling. While the former is well-studied, the latter has received less attention. We show that these are distinct phenomena with different implications for model design. Second, we develop a practical implementation using deep state-space models that scales to moderately large asset panels while remaining computationally tractable. Third, we provide evidence on when shared regime information is valuable and when it is not, offering guidance for practitioners on the appropriate scope of pooling.
