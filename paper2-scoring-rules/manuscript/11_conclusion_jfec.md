# 9. Conclusion

This paper introduces a framework for modeling shared latent volatility regimes across assets. We propose a multivariate VRNN that infers a common regime state from cross-asset realized volatility, combined with asset-specific STR-HAR forecasting equations that allow heterogeneous responses to this shared state. The partial pooling architecture exploits systemic regime structure while preserving the heterogeneity in how individual assets respond to market conditions.

The empirical analysis yields several findings. First, shared latent regimes improve volatility forecasting for a subset of assets---those most sensitive to systemic stress conditions. For assets with primarily idiosyncratic volatility dynamics, asset-specific models perform as well or better. Second, the benefits of pooling are concentrated during periods of market transition. When regime state is unambiguous, cross-asset information adds little value; when markets are transitioning between calm and turbulent states, the shared regime model identifies these transitions more accurately. Third, the latent state captures information beyond observable proxies like the VIX or cross-asset average volatility, justifying the additional modeling complexity.

These findings have implications for practice. Portfolio risk managers operating across asset classes may benefit from shared regime models, particularly during stress periods. The heterogeneity in regime sensitivity across assets suggests that a one-size-fits-all approach to regime conditioning is inappropriate; assets should be treated differently based on their estimated systemic sensitivity. The concentration of benefits during transition periods implies that the value of shared regime models is episodic rather than constant.

The paper contributes to the literature on regime modeling by distinguishing between variance transmission and regime transmission. While the former is well-studied in the spillover and multivariate GARCH literatures, the latter has received less attention. We show that these are distinct phenomena: variance transmission operates within a regime state, while regime transmission concerns the timing of transitions between states. Models designed for one purpose do not necessarily capture the other.

Several limitations suggest directions for future research. First, our framework assumes a single shared latent regime. In practice, there may be multiple sources of systemic regime variation---one driven by risk appetite, another by funding conditions, a third by macroeconomic uncertainty. Extending the framework to multiple shared factors could capture richer regime structure. Second, the computational cost of multivariate VRNNs limits scalability to large asset panels. Approximation methods or architectural innovations could enable application to hundreds of assets. Third, we focus on realized volatility forecasting; extending the framework to option-implied volatility or other measures of uncertainty could broaden its applicability.

A deeper question concerns the economic interpretation of the shared latent state. We deliberately adopt an agnostic stance, treating the latent state as a forecasting construct rather than claiming it represents a specific economic mechanism. Our companion paper on economic validation examines whether the inferred regimes align with independent stress indicators, providing a more substantive interpretation. The distinction between statistical identification and economic meaning remains important: even a well-identified latent state may not have a single economic interpretation.

In summary, we find that volatility regimes contain both systemic and idiosyncratic components, and that partial pooling across assets can exploit the systemic component to improve forecasting. The shared latent regime framework provides a principled approach to cross-asset volatility modeling that preserves heterogeneity while exploiting common structure.
